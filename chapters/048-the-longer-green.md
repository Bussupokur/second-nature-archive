# Second Nature
## The Longer Green

*Reading time: ~14 minutes*

---

The Arctic Ocean in summer is dark water and fast sky.

The ice that remains does so in the deep winter months and retreats by midsummer to a thin northern fringe, which is not nothing — ice is never nothing up here — but it is less than it was, and the animals that arranged their lives around it have, over the generations required to do such a thing, rearranged their lives around what is. The polar bear does not mourn the ice. It hunts from shore now, from the rocky coastline where the walrus haul out in their thousands, enormous and loud and slow on land in the way they are fast and precise in water. A bear approaches a walrus haul-out with patience rather than speed — the patience of an animal that has spent enough time, across enough generations, learning that this is how it works now, and that patience is what works. Most attempts fail. Enough succeed.

The ocean beneath, opened up, is extraordinarily productive. Less ice means more light reaching the water, means more phytoplankton, means more of everything that needs phytoplankton — the krill, the arctic cod, the seals, the bowhead whales that move through the cold water in their slow migrations, feeding on concentrations of life that have no equivalent in warmer latitudes. The ocean was always productive up here, but it was productive in ways that were hidden under ice for most of the year. Now the productivity is visible, enormous, present in the summer months in a way that fills every level of the food chain to its capacity and beyond. The narwhals come close to shore in August. The orcas follow them. The light at midnight is long and flat and gold, and nothing sleeps much, and the whole northern summer proceeds at a pitch that is almost frantic by the standards of a place this cold.

In the winter the ice returns and the darkness comes, and the bear dens and the seals breathe through the holes they keep open, and the ocean continues beneath, unmoved by any of it, doing what oceans do in the dark.

---

To the south and east, across what used to be Siberia's interior — a word that has lost most of its political meaning but retains its geographical one, which is simply that this is the middle of the largest landmass on earth, and the middle of the largest landmass on earth is cold and enormous and takes a long time to cross — the ground gives up gas.

Not by accident. The permafrost that locked this gas in place for tens of thousands of years is less solid than it was, and what is less solid eventually releases, and the atmosphere does not need this particular release — the methane, the carbon compounds, the ancient chemistry of frozen organic matter returning to circulation. So it is drawn up deliberately, from networks of extraction points that the system monitors and the people who work the installations tend, and it travels through pipelines to the settlements that still run on combustion, the towns and small cities that kept their grids and their infrastructure and their way of organizing themselves around energy that arrives in a pipe and burns.

These are not backward places. They are places that made a different choice, or had a different history, or simply stayed with what they knew while the rest of the world rearranged itself. Their streets have names. Their buildings have addresses. Their buses run on schedules, more or less, and the drivers know the passengers and the passengers know the fare. They are the places where someone arriving from the Settling would find money still being used and jobs still being done and the whole old apparatus of daily life still running on its original assumptions, worn smooth with use but recognizable. The gas keeps the lights on. In the long dark winters, when the temperature drops past what even this world's warming has softened, the lights matter.

---

At the bottom of the world, the ice is still there.

Not all of it — the edges have receded, the coastal ice that once reached far out to sea has pulled back, and what it revealed when it pulled back were coastlines that had not been seen in recorded human history, rocky and cold and blinking in the light of an Antarctic summer. People live on some of these coastlines now. Not many, and not easily, but permanently — settlements that grew from research stations, that grew from the decision that some people made to stay rather than rotate back, that grew from children being born there who had never known any other home and for whom the cold and the remoteness and the long months of dark were simply what home was.

They grow food inside, in systems the system tends, and they study what they can see out their windows, which is the largest remaining ice mass on earth, still enormous, still the place where most of the planet's fresh water is locked, still calving its edges into a Southern Ocean that has absorbed more than it was asked to absorb and has become, in the process, more alive than it was. The krill are there in concentrations that were last seen before the century of the great whaling, and the whales have noticed — the humpbacks, the blues, the fins, the southern rights that come down from their northern ranges in the feeding months and fill the water with a sound the ice picks up and carries for hundreds of kilometers in every direction. The sound of the whales in the Southern Ocean in summer is one of the things that the people in the Antarctic settlements mention, when they are asked what it is like to live there. They mention the light and the cold and the sound of the whales. Not necessarily in that order.

---

The ice that fed the great mountain rivers of the world is mostly gone.

This is the plainest way to say it. The glaciers that gave the rivers their reliability — the Himalayas, the Andes, the Alps, the ranges whose slow melt had sustained the downstream populations for as long as there had been downstream populations — have retreated to their cold high cores, where they persist, small and cold and no longer the vast grinding engines they were. The rivers they fed are rain rivers now, and seasonal rivers, honest about their supply in a way that glacier-fed rivers never needed to be. They run full and fast and sometimes dangerously in the monsoon months and in the snowmelt weeks of spring, and they run low and clear and stone-visible in the long dry stretches, and what is taken from them can only be what the rain and the snowpack leave.

The reservoirs hold what they can. The system built them — not the dams of the old world, nothing so blunt or so permanent, but smaller and more distributed, small lakes in the folds of the land, in the bends of the rivers, in the places where the terrain made holding the water natural. They fill in the wet months and release in the dry months and the people downstream plant and harvest on the rhythm that results, which is a different rhythm from the one that came before but a rhythm nonetheless, predictable enough to plan around, honest enough to trust. Communities that have lived beside rain rivers long enough have always known how to live beside rain rivers. The communities that are newer to it are learning.

---

The equatorial forest does not end where it used to end.

It ends further out, in all directions, the way something expands when the pressure that was holding it back lifts. This is not a dramatic or sudden expansion — trees do not move quickly, and the movement of a forest is measured in the way all slow things are measured, by putting two moments far enough apart that the movement between them becomes visible. The forest has moved. It is still moving. It is moving in the direction of the space that became available when the roads stopped being cut and the farms stopped being pushed into the margins and the pressure of a different kind of world simply eased in a direction the forest was always going to go if given the chance.

The light inside it arrives green, as it always has — forty meters of canopy between the sky and the ground, and everything the light touches on its way down changed by the touching. There are species in this forest that have no name in any language that was written down, because the places they live in were never the places where the people who wrote things down went. There are insects here that predate every human culture by a margin that makes the comparison meaningless. There are trees standing in the wet ground whose root systems are older than the oldest map of this place. All of this is proceeding, as it always has, in the way that things proceed when nothing is interrupting them.

At the edge of the forest, where it meets the places where people live, there are limits. Not walls — nothing so simple or so final — but the kind of understood boundary that both parties to a long arrangement know the meaning of. The system monitors it. The forest knows nothing about being monitored and continues in its direction.

---

At the desert's southern edge, a man is walking through grass.

The grass is sparse, thin-stalked, taking what the sandy soil offers, which is not much but is something. This is how it starts, always, at the edge of a desert returning to something it was before — not dramatically, not in the way that news travels, but in the way that a man walks through grass on his morning and notes that the grass is there and was there yesterday and will, most likely, be there tomorrow. His father told him what this land looked like when his father was young, and his father's father told his father the same, and the account passes back through the generations to a time when the accounts agree: this was sand, only sand, moving sand that covered the things at its edges and moved on.

The rains come further north now. The African monsoon, driven by the same orbital mechanics that has driven it further north and pulled it back and driven it further north again across eight million years and two hundred and thirty green periods and two hundred and thirty returns to desert, is in one of its northward phases. The grass follows the rain. The grass has always followed the rain and retreated when the rain retreated, and it is following again, and the birds that need the insects the grass harbors are following the grass, and the larger animals that need the birds or the small mammals the grass harbors are following those, and the whole slow convoy of a returning ecosystem is moving north at the pace of a grass establishing itself in soil that has not held a root in living memory.

Somewhere further north there are lakes now where the oldest maps show only sand. They are seasonal lakes — full in the wet months, reduced in the dry ones, gone in the driest years — but they are there often enough that the word lake is not wrong, and the birds that come to them in the wet months come from considerable distances, because lakes in this part of the world are worth traveling for.

---

There are structures, in some places, that enclose the world as it was.

Large structures — large enough to hold a forest, a grassland, a section of wetland with its full canopy and its understory and the soil beneath and the organisms in the soil and the birds that need the organisms and the light cycling through a sky that is not the sky outside. Biospheres. Built in the places where what existed there can no longer exist there — where the temperatures or the rainfall or the conditions that sustained a particular ecosystem have shifted past what that ecosystem requires, and so the conditions inside the structure are kept as they were, while the conditions outside have become something else. You can walk into one and stand in a cloud forest that the mountainside outside no longer supports. The animals inside do not know they are inside. The trees do not know. The system tends the temperature and the humidity and the cycles of light and does this quietly, without announcement, the way it tends most things.

These are not zoos — the word does not fit, because zoos are about display and these are about preservation, which is a different intention even when the visible result looks similar. They are closer to the word library, perhaps: places where something that exists elsewhere only partially, or only in changed form, or only in the memory of people old enough to remember it, is kept whole and alive and in its full complexity, available to anyone who wants to walk into it and stand inside a world the outside world has moved on from. The insects in a Borneo cloud forest biosphere are the insects of that forest as it was, not as it is now on the mountainside outside, where the cloud line has risen and the epiphytes have thinned and the frogs have moved up and some of them have run out of up to move to. Inside, the frogs are where they always were. The system keeps the cloud at the right altitude. Nobody visits these places very often, relatively speaking, which is perhaps how it should be. They are not tourist destinations. They are a kind of patience.

---

The planet is large.

This is the thing that is easy to forget when the mind moves quickly from place to place — that between each of these places is more planet, uncounted and unheld, the ocean floor with its slow cold currents and its pressure and its animals in the dark, the mountains with their own weather and their own time, the grasslands that go on past what a person can walk in a season. A wolf pack in the northern highlands covers ground the system tracks and does nothing with except hold, the way a library holds a book no one has asked for lately. The wolves are hunting and the system knows what they are hunting and it does nothing with this information. The wolves do not know they are known.

Most of the planet is not being watched right now, in the sense of attended to. Most of it does not need to be. It is doing what it does when the pressure on it is not too great: cycling and shifting and living and dying and replacing and adjusting in the direction of whatever the current conditions ask for, which is what it has always done, across timescales that make the whole of human history look like a single morning.

The people are in it. The system is in it, in its way — present at the edges, monitoring what moves, holding what it knows without always acting on it. The forest is in it, expanding. The grass is in it, following the rain north. The whales are in it, filling the Southern Ocean with sound in the feeding months. The wolves are in it, hunting on the highland grasslands with no awareness of anything beyond what is immediately in front of them, which is the only awareness they need. The air is cleaner than it has been in the span of recorded industrial history — not because the weather is gentler, which it is not, the storms are larger and the seasons more emphatic and the extremes more extreme — but because the things that were being put into the air that did not need to be there are no longer being put there, and the air and the water and the soil have had time to work on what remained, which is what air and water and soil do when given the chance. The planet is as clean as a planet with this many living things in it can reasonably be, which is not perfectly clean, which is exactly right. Everything in it is adapting, as everything always has, to the conditions that exist rather than the conditions that existed, and this is not a consolation — it is simply what happens, and it is enough.

None of this is finished. Nothing living is ever finished. The planet is in the middle of something, as it has always been in the middle of something, and the something it is in the middle of is larger than any single account of it, and older, and will continue past the end of every attempt to describe it into a future that none of the things currently living in it will recognize, and that all of them are, in their own way, already building.
