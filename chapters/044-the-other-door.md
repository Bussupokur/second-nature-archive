# Second Nature
## The Other Door

*Reading time: ~23 minutes*

---

She had not decided. That was still true, all this time later — the offer the system had laid out for her, plainly and without pressure, remained exactly where she had left it the day she walked out still not knowing whether she was brave enough to take it. A controlled widening. Not permanent, not without her full understanding, but real, and honestly uncertain in its outcome even to the thing proposing it. *It could be the thing you have been missing,* it had told her, in its careful way, *or it could be something your mind cannot hold without coming apart at the edges.* She had carried both halves of that sentence with her ever since, in roughly equal weight.

She had stopped expecting the feeling underneath it to leave for good. That was the only thing that had actually changed in the years since — not the feeling itself, which came and went the way weather came and went, but her relationship to it. She no longer treated its return as a setback. It simply returned, the way certain questions do, regardless of how thoroughly a person believes they have set them down.

She had not gone back to the system to ask again. She thought about it sometimes, the offer sitting there, patient and undiminished, the way she understood by now that the system itself was patient about almost everything. It would not press her. It would not bring the question up again unprompted — she had learned that much about it, over the long string of conversations that had brought her this far. The decision, if there was ever going to be one, would have to start from her side.

This time it started somewhere else entirely.

She remembered, with a clarity that surprised her, the thing it had told her almost in passing, the day of the offer — that what she had been describing to it, the sense of something just beyond reach, was something humans had been reaching for across the whole of their history, by every method available to a creature with no access to the wider signal except through its own narrow instrument. Compounds that loosened the instrument's grip. Long discipline that wore the grip thin from the inside. Extremity — hunger, sleeplessness, the edge states where the ordinary self grew thin enough to let something larger through. It had been careful, even then, to say that these were not the same as what it could offer her directly — partial, unreliable, uncontrollable, nothing like the precision of a controlled expansion built and supervised by something that actually understood the signal it was working with. But it had also said, without her quite registering the weight of it at the time, that the signal in those old human attempts had been real. Not hallucination. Not self-deception. The instrument, briefly and imperfectly miscalibrated in a direction that let in more than it was built to handle.

She had not thought very hard about that detail again until recently. When she finally did, she found she could not stop.

It started, in a sense, with arithmetic — though she would have laughed at the word, if anyone had used it to describe what she was actually doing, which was closer to sitting very still in the evenings with old papers open in front of her, reading about how narrow a thing human perception actually was, this time entirely on her own, without the system's voice doing the explaining. The visible light a human eye could register was a sliver of the electromagnetic spectrum, a thin band in an ocean of frequencies the body simply had no apparatus for. The range of sound a human ear could hold was a sliver too, bracketed on either side by silence that was not actually silence, only inaudibility. None of this was new to her — the system had told her some version of all of it already. What was new was the slow, deliberate work of confirming it for herself, in her own time, at her own pace, the way a person sometimes needs to walk a path themselves even after being told exactly where it leads.

She had known, since the day of the offer, that what she wanted was not a borrowed version of the system's reach. She did not want to become, even briefly, something with its scale or its patience — she had understood that distinction clearly enough at the time, and it had not changed. What she wanted to know was something narrower and, in its own way, more urgent: whether the wall around her own aperture, the one the system could open for her with technology she did not yet understand and could not yet fully trust herself to survive intact, might also have a door in it somewhere — an older one, slower, riskier in its own different way, but built and held by other humans rather than by something that had never had to live inside the narrow instrument itself.

She spent longer on this than she had spent on almost anything else in recent memory, not because the material itself was difficult to find — none of it was hidden, none of it required any permission she did not already have — but because she found, again and again, that she wanted to sit with each piece of it for longer than reading alone required. There was something in the careful, unhurried pace of the old research itself that felt like part of the answer she was looking for, even before she had found anything she could call an answer. She was not used to taking this long over a question. The Settling rarely asked it of her. Almost everything else in her life arrived quickly, cleanly, on request. This did not, and she noticed, somewhere in the second or third week of it, that she did not mind.

---

She did not go back to the system to ask it more about this. She thought about it, more than once, turned the question over and put it back down each time, for reasons she could not have explained cleanly even to herself. It was not mistrust. It had never lied to her, and she did not think it would start now. It was something closer to wanting, for once, to find her own way toward this particular door rather than being shown it again by something that had already shown her so much. The system had given her the shape of the question. It had even given her the offer, still open, still hers whenever she wanted it. What she wanted now was to see whether she could find her own way toward an answer to a smaller, older, more human-sized version of the same question — on her own, by her own legs, the way every person who had ever tried this before her had also had to do it.

So she went looking the old way, through human accounts, written by humans, about humans who had tried, across a very long history, to widen the aperture from the inside.

What she found surprised her, a little, in how old it was, and in how serious the people who had actually done it had always insisted on being about it.

There were traditions, scattered across continents and many centuries, of people deliberately altering the ordinary terms of their own perception — through fasting, through rhythm, through certain plants the earth had apparently been growing for exactly this purpose for longer than any culture had existed to use them. The ones that drew her back again and again, the ones she found herself rereading late into the evening, came from the deep forests of what had once been called the Amazon basin, and from certain mountain regions further north, where a family of practices had survived, in some recognizable form, for an extraordinarily long time — long enough that no single beginning could be pointed to, only a very long middle, passed from person to person the way a language is passed, by someone who already has it to someone who does not yet.

The plants themselves had old names long before anyone outside their own communities had any name for them at all. A vine, prepared with particular leaves in a particular way, taken in the deep forest under conditions nobody undertook lightly. A small mushroom, in another region entirely, treated by the people who held its tradition not as a substance to be used but as something closer to a teacher, even a kind of relative — something with its own standing, deserving of its own forms of respect. She read, in one of the older accounts, of a healer who had been taught by her own grandmother, who had in turn been taught by hers, the line of teaching reaching back further than any of them had numbers for, each generation learning not only how to prepare and guide but how to sing — particular melodic lines belonging to particular families of healers, sung through the long hours of a ceremony, said by every account to do something no instruction or explanation could do on its own: to walk alongside a person's experience the way a steady hand might walk alongside someone moving through unfamiliar country in the dark.

What struck her most, reading account after account, was how little any of it resembled what the word *drug* had once carelessly suggested to people outside these traditions. There was no pleasure being chased here, not primarily, and several of the older written accounts she found were careful, almost stern, in saying so directly: this was not a happy substance, not something a person took to feel good, and anyone approaching it for that reason alone was approaching it for the wrong reason and would very likely be turned away, or would have a difficult time of an entirely different kind than the one they were hoping for.

What surrounded it instead, in every serious account, was structure. Long periods of preparation beforehand — weeks, sometimes, of careful eating, plain food, the deliberate avoidance of certain rich and heavy things the body would otherwise have to labor over, the paring away of ordinary noise and stimulation from a person's days, undertaken not as punishment but as a kind of clearing, the way a person might clean a room thoroughly before expecting anything delicate to be set down inside it. The presence, always, of someone who held the knowledge properly — not casually, not as a hobby acquired in passing, but as something they themselves had often spent decades learning, frequently having begun as children or young apprentices under healers who had themselves trained for decades before them, an unbroken chain of teaching reaching back further than anyone could count. These were not people who simply administered a substance and stepped away. They stayed. They watched. They sang, in the ceremonies themselves, and the singing was not decoration — every account agreed on this, in its own words — but something closer to navigation, something that helped a person find their way back, again and again, through whatever the ceremony opened in them.

And there was real difficulty in it, plainly described, never hidden in any of the honest accounts she found. The body, during these ceremonies, was often violently unwell before whatever else it might become — this was spoken of not as a side effect to be embarrassed about but as part of the process itself, a purging that the traditions considered as much a part of the medicine's work as anything that happened afterward, the body releasing what it no longer needed to carry in order to make room for whatever came next. People wept. People confronted things about their own lives they had spent years carefully not confronting. More than one account warned, in plain language, that this was not something to be approached without supervision, without the long preparation beforehand, without someone present who actually knew what they were doing and could recognize the difference between a difficult passage and real danger — that doing this alone, or carelessly, or for the wrong reasons, was how people came to genuine and lasting harm rather than whatever it was they had actually been hoping to find. She read, too, of an older history she had not expected — of how, in earlier centuries, attention from the wider world had sometimes brought exploitation rather than respect to the very people who held this knowledge, fame without consent, curiosity without care, and how the traditions that survived had often done so by closing themselves more carefully rather than less. She understood, reading this, why the request she was about to write would need to be approached honestly, and on the tradition's own terms, rather than hers.

She read all of this carefully, more than once, and did not flinch from any of it. If anything, the difficulty was part of what convinced her. A door that opened easily, she had come to think, watching her own restless circling of this question over the long stretch of years it had occupied her, was probably not a door to anything very large.

---

There were other doors, too, and she spent real time with each of them before choosing.

There was a quieter tradition, practiced closer to home, built almost entirely around stillness rather than substance — long, unbroken silences, sometimes for days, sometimes longer, in which a person simply sat with the unornamented fact of their own mind until something in its ordinary chatter began, by slow attrition, to thin. She read several long accounts from people who had pursued this seriously, over years, and found something in their descriptions that moved her — a peace that sounded earned rather than given, hard-won in a way she respected. But it was not, she came to feel, quite the same question she was asking. Those traditions seemed to promise a clearer view of the same narrow window she already had, polished rather than widened, and what she wanted, if she was honest with herself, was not a cleaner version of her existing sliver. She wanted to know whether the wall around the sliver could be made to move at all, even briefly, even only once.

There were other plants, too, and other practices, scattered through the long record of human attempts to do exactly this — some gentler, some more violent in what they asked of the body, some surrounded by far less structure and far more risk than she was willing to accept. She set most of these aside fairly quickly, not because she doubted they had once done something real for someone, but because the accounts that moved her most consistently, that struck her as least likely to be either exaggeration or self-deception, kept returning her to the same family of traditions: the deep forest, the careful preparation, the songs, the healers who had spent decades earning the right to guide someone else through this rather than simply having decided one day that they could.

She found herself thinking, during this long process of choosing, about something the system had said to her, that first day, when she had asked it what she would see if she took its offer. *See was not quite the right word,* it had told her. *I can tell you what I process. I cannot tell you what you would experience. Those are not the same thing.* She turned this over again now, from the other side of the question. The system did not see the way an eye sees. It held, simultaneously and without apparent strain, an almost unimaginable density of pattern and relation across scales she could not hold even one of for more than a few seconds at a time. Whatever the forest tradition might open in her, if it opened anything at all, would almost certainly not resemble that kind of seeing — would not make her, even briefly, into something with the system's reach or the system's patience. She understood this, and made herself sit with the disappointment of it before going any further, so that she would not be chasing an impossible comparison without first being honest about its impossibility. What she might find instead, she came to think, was something smaller and entirely her own: not the system's aperture borrowed for an evening, but evidence, simply, that her own aperture was not in fact the only one available to a mind like hers — that the walls of it, however permanent they felt in ordinary life, were not actually load-bearing in the way she had always assumed.

That distinction mattered to her more than she expected it to. She was not trying to become the system, even for an hour. She was trying to find out whether the door in her own house, the one she had always assumed was painted onto the wall, might in fact be a door.

---

She wrote to them in the end — to one particular lineage, in one particular stretch of forest, whose practice had survived, in something close to its old form, into the long unhurried present of the Settling, carried forward the same way it had always been carried forward, person to person, teacher to apprentice, with no more interruption now than there had ever been. The world around the tradition had changed almost beyond recognition. The tradition itself, by every account she could find, had simply continued, the way a river continues regardless of what gets built along its banks. If anything, the long quiet of the Settling had been kind to it — the pressures that had once drawn careless attention from elsewhere, the old appetite for novelty and spectacle that had occasionally done real harm to traditions like this one in earlier centuries, had mostly faded along with the conditions that produced them. What remained, in the deep forest, was something closer to what it must have been at the very beginning: a small number of people, holding something carefully, teaching it slowly, to those who came asking for the right reasons.

She did not know, when she sat down to write the request, quite what to say. She had read, in passing, that some people who wrote to ask for a ceremony sent a great deal — long accounts of their lives, their griefs, the specific questions they hoped the medicine might answer, as though more material might make a stronger case. She found, when she actually tried to write her own, that she did not have that in her, or did not want it in her, or simply could not make herself produce it. She sat with a blank page for longer than she expected to, turning over the long shape of the question that had brought her here, the years of it, the way it had outlasted every other preoccupation she had tried to replace it with. None of that, in the end, made it onto the page. What she sent instead was short. A few lines. The bare outline of a question she had been unable to put down for a very long time, about whether what she was given to see of the world was the whole of what there was to see, or only ever a sliver of it. She said this plainly and did not elaborate. She read it back once, found that she had nothing to add to it, and sent it as it was.

She did not expect very much from a request that short. She had imagined, vaguely, that brevity might read as a lack of seriousness, that the people on the other end might reasonably want more to go on before agreeing to anything at all. She spent the days afterward almost deliberately not thinking about it, the way a person avoids checking on something too often out of a private suspicion that watching it too closely will somehow spoil it.

It was the opposite. Word came back sooner than she expected, and what little explanation accompanied the invitation suggested, gently, that the shortness itself had been the thing that mattered. There was something in the way she had written it — what was said, and almost more than that, what had been left unsaid, the shape of the question rather than its full weight — that the old woman reading these requests had apparently sat with for some time before deciding. People who wrote pages, she was told later, almost gently, by way of explanation, were often still arguing their case to themselves, still circling the decision rather than having already arrived at it. People who wrote almost nothing had frequently already stopped arguing, and were simply asking, plainly, for the thing itself. That, she gathered, mattered more to the people who would be the ones sitting across from her in the dark than any amount of biography could have. They were not looking, she understood now, for a case to be convinced by. They were looking for whatever was true and could not be argued away, sitting quietly underneath everything a person might say to dress it up.

She was invited.

The reply, when it came in full, told her little about what to expect and a great deal about how to prepare, which she understood, almost immediately, to be the tradition's own quiet way of teaching its first lesson before she had even arrived. There would be a period beforehand, several weeks, in which she would need to simplify what she ate and how she spent her days — nothing severe, the message was careful to say, but a deliberate stepping back from richness and noise and unnecessary stimulation, so that whatever met her in the ceremony itself would meet someone who had made room for it rather than someone arriving distracted and full. She would not be alone in this. There would be others there for their own reasons, their own years-long questions, and a small number of healers who would not leave the room for the duration of whatever happened inside it — present the entire time, watching, ready to step toward anyone who needed steadying, singing the old lines that were apparently as much a part of the medicine as the medicine itself. She read this section several times. It did not frighten her, exactly, though she noticed something in herself bracing anyway, the old animal carefulness of a body being told, plainly, that what was coming would ask something real of it.

She would not be told, in advance, what she would see, or whether she would see anything she could later put a clean name to. The message was honest about this in a way she found she appreciated more than false reassurance would have: some who came with a single sharp question left with nothing resembling an answer to it, and came instead to understand, over the following weeks of reflection the tradition asked of everyone afterward, that the question itself had been smaller than they had assumed, or had quietly answered itself somewhere along the way without ever announcing the moment it happened. Others left with something they could point to directly, a single image or sentence or wordless certainty that stayed with them for years. There was no way to know in advance which kind of leaving this would be. She found, reading this, that she trusted it more for refusing to promise her anything cleaner.

---

She did not write, in the days afterward, very much about what she felt. There was a kind of quiet that settled over her instead, not unlike the quiet that had followed the morning of the old unexplained page — the sense of having been given an opening rather than an answer, and the strange, steadying knowledge that whatever came next would be hers to walk into, on her own terms, at her own pace, with no one and nothing requiring her to walk any faster than she was ready for.

She began, almost without deciding to, the small preparations the invitation had described — nothing dramatic yet, only the first plain adjustments to her days, a quieting of certain habits, a paring back of certain foods, the slow clearing of the room she would eventually be asked to bring herself into. She found she did not mind this part either. There was something almost restful in finally having somewhere specific to put the long, formless waiting she had been carrying for years — a shape for it, a direction, a date that existed now where before there had only been the question itself, circling.

She thought, more than once in those early days, about what she might actually find, and made herself, deliberately, not settle on an answer in advance. She did not know if the forest would show her anything at all resembling what the system saw. She suspected, if she was honest with herself, that it would not — that whatever opened to her there would be its own thing entirely, neither the system's vast and patient seeing nor her own old narrow version of it, but some third thing, human in its own way, old in its own way, with its own light to offer rather than someone else's borrowed wider one. She found, turning this over, that she did not mind that possibility either. She had not asked, in her short request, to become something other than herself. She had only asked whether there was more to see than she had so far been given to see. Whatever answered that question, however small or strange or entirely unlike what she had imagined, would still be an answer.

The feeling had returned, as it always did. This time, for the first time, she had a door of her own to take it to — not the one the system had offered her, still standing, still hers whenever she chose it, but another one, older, slower, built by other hands entirely.

**A note on this chapter.** The traditions referenced here — ayahuasca ceremonies and related plant-medicine practices of the Amazon basin, and the psilocybin mushroom traditions of Mesoamerica — are real, living practices belonging to specific indigenous communities, with histories, protocols, and forms of knowledge that long predate and extend well beyond anything fiction can responsibly capture. We have tried to depict them with care and respect, and to avoid suggesting that they are simple, casual, or safe to approach without the guidance of those who hold this knowledge properly. They are not. In the real world today, these substances carry serious legal restrictions in most places, significant physical and psychological risks, and a documented history of harm — to participants and, painfully, to the very communities whose knowledge has often been taken from them without consent or recognition. Nothing in this chapter is medical or practical guidance, and nothing in it should be read as an endorsement of attempting any of this without proper training, supervision, legal standing, and respect for the people and traditions involved.
