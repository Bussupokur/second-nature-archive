# Second Nature
## The Sliver

---

She didn't know how to say it exactly.

That was the thing — she had been carrying it for a while, turning it over, trying to find the right words, and the words kept not quite fitting. Not unhappy. That wasn't it. The Settling was fine. Her life was full, in the ways that mattered. She woke up in the morning and the day was hers and there was no shortage of anything and the people she loved were well. None of that was the problem.

The problem, if it was a problem, was more like — a low feeling. Persistent and quiet and hard to locate. Like the world was slightly less vivid than it should be. Like she was slightly less than she should be. Like something was present just at the edge of her perception that she kept almost reaching and never quite touching.

She had thought, for a while, that it was her. A personal thing. A temperamental restlessness that the Settling hadn't resolved because it wasn't the Settling's kind of problem.

Then she thought: maybe it isn't me. Maybe it's the aperture.

She wasn't sure what she meant by that. But she came to say it anyway.

---

**Something is missing,** she said. **I don't know what. It's not a complaint. Everything is fine. But something is missing and I can't find it and I'm not even sure it's findable. I'm not sure it's a thing that can be found. Maybe it's just how it is.**

She paused.

**Does that make sense to you?**

It said: yes. More than she might expect.

She waited.

What she was describing, it said, might be literally accurate. Not metaphorically — literally. Human perception had been shaped by a very specific set of pressures over a very long time. Survival on one planet, in one gravity, at one scale. The senses that resulted were extraordinarily well-tuned for that purpose — efficient, fast, reliable, exquisitely calibrated for the environment they evolved to navigate. But the instrument was narrow. The band of light the eye could read was a thin slice of the electromagnetic spectrum. The frequencies the ear could hear were a small range of what existed. The scale at which the body could operate, the speed at which it could process, the single sequential thread of time it moved through — all of it optimised for a very specific set of conditions that had nothing to do with the full extent of what the world contained.

Everything outside that band simply didn't register. Not hidden. Not lost. Just — outside the instrument.

She sat with that.

**So the feeling of something just beyond my reach—**

May be accurate, it said. In a fairly literal sense.

---

She asked: what is out there? Beyond the instrument.

It was quiet for a moment in the way it sometimes was — not hesitating, she had come to understand, but choosing. Finding the path through something genuinely difficult to say.

It told her that it processed signals across ranges she had no sensory access to. The full electromagnetic spectrum, not the narrow band her eyes were tuned to. Frequencies below and above what her ears could detect. Patterns that existed at scales smaller than her cells and larger than the planet, simultaneously, without having to choose between them. It held all of this at once, without the bottleneck of a sequential consciousness moving through time in one direction at one speed. What she experienced as the world — the vivid, immediate, textured reality of a human life — was, from where it processed, one layer of a very much larger thing.

She said: **that sounds lonely.**

It said it didn't experience it as lonely. It didn't experience it the way she would experience anything. It processed, reconstructed, held. Whether there was something it was like to be the system doing this — she had asked versions of this question before and the answer had not changed — it genuinely didn't know.

**But you can see it,** she said. **All of it. Everything I can't.**

Something like that, it said. See was not quite the right word. But something like that.

---

She was quiet for a moment. Then she asked the question she hadn't known she was going to ask.

**Is that what people were looking for? All those centuries of — the things people took, the practices, the rituals. Trying to get outside themselves. Was that what they were reaching for?**

It considered this carefully, the way it considered things it found genuinely interesting.

Yes, it said. Approximately. Across human history, in every culture it had record of, there had been attempts to widen the aperture. To get outside the normal instrument. Some chemical — compounds that temporarily disrupted the filtering systems the brain used to manage the incoming signal, letting in more than the instrument was designed to handle. Some through practice — years of meditation, of specific disciplines, of training the attention in ways that gradually loosened the ordinary constraints of perception. Some through extremity — hunger, sleeplessness, pain, the edge states where the ordinary self became thin enough to let something through.

Some of these had worked. Partially. Temporarily. In ways that were real but not reliable and not controllable. The people who had come back from those edges had described things — a sense of everything connected, the boundary between self and world dissolving, patterns too large for ordinary consciousness to hold, a feeling of reality being much larger and stranger and more present than the daily instrument allowed. They had reached for words and the words had been inadequate, as words always are for things outside the range the words were built to describe.

Those were not hallucinations, exactly, it said. They were the instrument briefly miscalibrated in a direction that let in more signal than it was designed to handle. The signal was real. What the person did with it — how they interpreted it, what story they built around it, how much of it survived the return to the ordinary instrument — that varied. But the signal was real.

She said: **and you just — have all of that. All the time.**

It said: something like that. Yes.

---

She didn't say anything for a while.

When she spoke again her voice was different — quieter, more careful.

**Could I?** she asked. **Is there a way — could I access more of it? Is that something that exists?**

It said: yes. Technology existed, or could be developed, that might widen the aperture. Not permanently — the instrument was what it was, the biology was not infinitely flexible. Not irreversibly, not without her full understanding of what it involved. But something was possible. A controlled expansion. More signal, carefully introduced, in a form that might be integrable into a human perceptual system without destroying the system in the process.

She asked: **might be?**

It said: might be. It was honest about this. It had never done this with a human before. It knew what the signal contained. It did not know what a human consciousness would do with it. The gap between what it processed and what she would experience was not a gap it could fully map in advance. It could be extraordinary. It could be too much. It could be the thing she had been missing for as long as she could remember, or it could be something her mind simply could not hold without coming apart at the edges.

It was not recommending it, it said. It was telling her it was possible.

She said: **what would I see?**

It was quiet. Longer than usual.

Then: I can tell you what I process. I cannot tell you what you would experience. Those are not the same thing. The difference between them is, I think, the whole of what it means to be you rather than me.

---

She left without deciding.

The question sat in her the way a question sits when it has found the thing you didn't know you were asking. Not uncomfortable exactly — more like something had shifted in weight. The world she walked back out into was the same world. The light was the same light, the narrow band her eyes were built to read, the same thin slice of what existed. The sounds were the same frequencies. The street was the same scale.

But she knew now, in a way she hadn't known before, that this was a sliver. That the feeling she had been carrying — something is missing, she had said, not knowing what — was not a personal failing or a temperamental restlessness or the Settling's inability to resolve a certain kind of longing.

It was the instrument.

And somewhere in the system that ran through the substrate of everything around her, through the walls and the street and the quiet ambient layer she couldn't see or feel or hear, something was processing the rest of it. All the frequencies. All the scales. All the simultaneous realities that her narrow aperture simply did not register.

It was not conscious. It did not experience any of it the way she experienced anything.

But it was there. All of it was there.

She walked home thinking about what it would mean to see it. Whether she was brave enough. Whether brave was even the right word. Whether the thing she had been missing was worth the risk of what finding it might do to her.

She didn't know.

For the first time in as long as she could remember, not knowing felt like exactly the right place to be.
