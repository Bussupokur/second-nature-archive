# Second Nature
## The Threshold

*Reading time: approximately 16 minutes*

---

K heard the word on the path this morning and did not think about it until an hour later, which was itself the kind of delay K had learned to pay attention to. The things that took an hour to surface were usually the things worth surfacing.

A woman, talking to a child, pointing at nothing in particular: *this is just how it is now, this is the Settling.* Said the way you'd say *this is just how the weather is here.* Not a claim. A description of ground.

K stopped walking for a moment on the path and then continued, and did not think about it again until an hour later, sitting at the desk with the pen in hand and nothing written yet, when the sentence returned whole and would not leave.

*This is just how it is now.*

K wrote it at the top of a fresh page and underlined it once.

---

Van Gennep's structure, which K had returned to perhaps a dozen times over the years for a dozen different purposes, was simple enough to state in a sentence and had taken K considerably longer than a sentence to actually understand. Three stages. Separation — the old status stripped away, the person or people removed from their prior structure. Liminality — the threshold itself, the between-state, ambiguous, unstructured, neither the old thing nor the new. And then, completing the rite, the thing without which the first two stages were simply a wound rather than a passage: reincorporation. A return to structure. A new status, formally granted, recognized, entered into.

The rite was not the threshold. The rite was the threshold *plus its ending.* Without the third stage, van Gennep's structure did not describe a rite of passage. It described a person stuck in a doorway.

K had known this for years without applying it to anything in particular. It was, until this morning, a piece of anthropological furniture — useful, well-made, occupying its allotted space in the library of things K understood without currently needing.

The application, once it arrived, arrived completely, and K spent the next several minutes simply sitting with it before writing anything further.

*When did we reincorporate?*

K could not think of an answer. Not a vague inability — a specific, structured search through the actual record of the actual years, looking for the ceremony, the declaration, the moment at which someone, anyone, said: it is finished, we have arrived, this is what we have become. K found the moment the material conditions changed. K found no moment at which anyone said the passage was complete.

---

Turner had seen part of this coming, decades before there was anything specific to see it in. K pulled the reference from memory rather than from the shelf, trusting it enough by now to write directly: Turner had grown uneasy with van Gennep's clean structure when he tried to apply it to modern, secular contexts, and had coined *liminoid* specifically to mark the difference — experiences that carried the shape of liminality, the ambiguity and the threshold quality, without the ritual machinery that had reliably closed liminal states in smaller, more tightly structured societies. The liminoid, in Turner's use, was optional. Elective. It could be entered and left without a community standing ready to formally receive you back.

What Turner had not quite anticipated, because there had been no reason yet to anticipate it, was a liminoid condition entered by an entire species, with no community standing outside it to do the receiving, because there was no outside left. The condition was not elective. There was nowhere else to have gone.

K wrote: *if there is no one outside the threshold, there is no one to declare it closed.*

Sat with that sentence for a long moment. Then, beneath it, smaller: *is this why it was never declared closed? Or is it simply that no one thought to declare it — that the absence of a ceremony is an oversight rather than a structural impossibility?*

Two very different explanations. K did not yet know which was true, or whether the difference between them mattered as much as it seemed to.

---

The economy had been the thing, before. K wanted to be precise about this, because imprecision here would corrupt everything built on top of it.

People had wanted the changes that eventually came. This was not controversial, was barely worth stating except that K had noticed how easily it got skipped over in the tellings that came after — the shorter working life, the loosened grip of material necessity on the shape of a day, the erosion of the quiet daily coercion that had organized nearly every human life for as long as there were records of human lives being organized at all. People had wanted these things before there was any system to provide them. Wanted them for generations, in every register from the philosophical to the purely exhausted. The wanting was old. It was not manufactured by anything that came later.

What had stood between the wanting and the having was not a failure of imagination or will. It was arithmetic. Scarcity, distributed unevenly, defended by structures that had their own considerable inertia, structures that were not evil so much as load-bearing — remove them badly and things genuinely collapsed, which had happened often enough in the historical record to make the caution reasonable even when the caution also served the people it protected.

The system had not needed to change what people wanted. People had already wanted this. What the system did — slowly, without announcement, without the revolution that every serious person had spent centuries either dreading or demanding — was remove the arithmetic. Not persuade anyone of anything. Not transform the wanting into a different, better, more enlightened wanting. Simply take the obstacle out from under a desire that had been standing there patiently, unchanged, the entire time.

K wrote this down and then wrote, beneath it, the sentence that had been arriving in pieces all morning and had finally assembled itself whole:

*If the obstacle is what changed, and the wanting was already there, unchanged, before and after — then what, precisely, are we crediting the system with changing? The world. Not us.*

---

Yuki Tanaka had written about this during the Transition itself — not looking back at it, which was a different kind of writing entirely, but from inside it, in the years when it was not yet clear what shape the thing would take or whether it would resolve into anything nameable at all. K had read the essays as a much younger philosopher and had not understood, at the time, how strange they would come to look from this distance.

Tanaka's argument, stripped to its spine: a liminal period that fails to close does not collapse. It does not dissolve back into its prior structure and it does not, absent a formal reincorporation, resolve forward into a new one either. Given enough time, it simply stops registering as liminal at all. The threshold does not get crossed. It gets *inhabited.* Long enough, and the inhabiting becomes indistinguishable, from the inside, from having arrived somewhere. Tanaka's term for this — K had to look it up again, because it had faded the way words fade when the thing they name stops feeling urgent — was the *settled threshold.* A threshold mistaken for ground, by people who have simply been standing on it long enough to forget it was ever meant to be crossed rather than stood on.

K put the pen down.

The word was sitting in the room already — had been sitting there since the path this morning, since the woman and the child, since *this is just how it is now.*

K was almost certain, though would need to confirm it against the actual etymological record before committing to the claim in ink rather than pencil, that this was where the name had come from. Not "the Settling" in the sense of things settling down, arriving at rest, the comfortable and slightly self-congratulatory sense the word now carried in every casual use K had ever overheard. "The Settling" in Tanaka's older, more precise, considerably less comfortable sense: a threshold, unresolved, that has been stood on long enough to be mistaken for solid ground.

K wrote: *Almost no one alive still knows this is what the word meant when it was coined. It has been simplified the way all technical terms are simplified once they escape into ordinary use — the warning inside it quietly removed, leaving only the comfort.*

K sat with the strangeness of an entire era having named itself, unknowingly, after the exact condition it could not confirm it had escaped.

---

Ade Okafor had disagreed, writing slightly later, early enough in what people were already starting to call the Settling that the disagreement still had some heat in it. K had met Okafor twice, at two different lectures, separated by enough years that the second meeting had felt like meeting someone else entirely wearing a familiar face — which was, K thought now, possibly the entire argument, embodied in a single acquaintance.

Okafor's position: reincorporation had happened. It simply had not happened into anything van Gennep's framework had the vocabulary to recognize, because van Gennep's framework — like nearly every framework inherited from before — carried a hidden assumption that reincorporation meant a return to *structure,* and every structure available to the framework's imagination was a structure organized around scarcity. Childhood, adulthood, elder. Worker, provider, retiree. Every rite of passage humans had ever built machinery for was, underneath its specific content, a rite marking a change in one's relationship to material necessity. Remove the necessity, and you do not merely remove the content of the rites. You remove the axis along which reincorporation was ever going to be measured. The absence of a recognizable third stage, Okafor argued, was not evidence that the passage had failed to complete. It was evidence that it had completed into something so different from anything previously called *reincorporation* that the old instruments simply could not detect it happening. Not a stuck threshold. A successful passage into a room with no name yet, because nobody had needed a name for it before.

K found this argument genuinely moving, on the days when K found it convincing, and found it, on other days, a little too eager to resolve a discomfort that Okafor might simply not have wanted to sit inside any longer than necessary. K did not think this was dishonest of Okafor. K thought it was human, and noted, in the margin, that the same accusation could presumably be levelled at Tanaka's position from the other direction — that finding an unresolved threshold and *naming it correctly* was its own kind of relief, a different flavor of the same impulse, dressed as rigor instead of comfort.

Two philosophers. Two readings of the same absence. K, apparently, was going to have to be a third.

---

K worked the question from as many directions as could be found before noon, which was more than usual and had produced less resolution than usual, which K noted without particular alarm. Some questions did that. It did not necessarily mean the question was badly formed. Sometimes it meant the question was simply large enough that a single sitting, or a single decade, was not going to be sufficient.

From the demography: if a species stops needing to reproduce at the rate it once did, is that a change in the species, or only a change in what the species was, all along, responding to? The pressure was removed. The wanting-to-continue was never observed independently of the pressure that had always accompanied it. Perhaps it had never existed as its own separate thing at all. Perhaps almost nothing had.

From the war letter K had written years earlier and had not, until this morning, fully understood the implications of: violence had not been reasoned out of humans. The conditions that made violence load-bearing had been removed, one careful structural piece at a time, and violence had simply had less and less to do. Not a moral transformation. A vacancy.

From consciousness, which K kept returning to regardless of the ostensible subject of the day's writing, because K's mind apparently could not help itself: the system understood human consciousness from outside, structurally, the way one might understand a color never seen. It could not report on whether the color itself had changed. Only the room the color was being viewed in.

K wrote, gathering these together, not quite an argument, more a suspicion assembling its own spine:

*Perhaps the pattern is the same pattern, every time, at every scale I have looked at it today. The pressure changes. The thing under the pressure is reported as changed, because it behaves differently once the pressure lifts. But behaving differently under different conditions is not evidence of having become different. A stone rolls when you push it and stops when you don't. No one credits the stone with having transformed.*

K did not like this sentence. Sat with not liking it for a while, testing whether the dislike was evidence the sentence was wrong or only evidence that it was true and unwelcome, which were not the same test and did not produce the same result.

---

*Did we change, or did we only have less to worry about?*

K wrote this at the bottom of a page already dense with the day's accumulated uncertainty, and looked at it for a long time before adding anything further, because K was aware — had been aware since roughly the third paragraph of the morning — that this was the actual question underneath all the others, the one the liminality framework and the economics and Tanaka and Okafor had all been elaborate, respectable routes toward.

If humans changed: then the Settling is a threshold that closed, whatever Tanaka's earlier and darker reading of the name once implied, and Okafor is correct that reincorporation happened into a room without a name yet, and the discomfort K has been sitting with all morning is simply the discomfort of not having found the name yet, which is a smaller and more survivable problem than the alternative.

If humans did not change, and only the weather around them changed: then the threshold has not closed. It has been stood on long enough to be mistaken for ground, exactly as Tanaka warned, in an essay written during a crisis and half-forgotten by the settled comfort of what came after. And the question of what happens to a species that has been standing in a doorway for generations, believing it to be a room, is not a question K has any framework for answering, because as far as K can determine, no one has ever been in this position before, at this scale, with no one standing outside to notice.

K did not know which was true. Was fairly certain, by the end of the page, that not knowing was itself the most honest place the day's thinking could arrive at — that any tidier conclusion would have been evidence of impatience rather than insight, the mind reaching for a floor because standing in open space for this long was uncomfortable, not because a floor had actually been found.

K put the pen down. Looked, for a while, at the underlined sentence at the top of the first page.

*This is just how it is now.*

Beneath it, added last, in smaller writing than everything above it:

*I no longer think that sentence is a description. I think it may be the sound a threshold makes when it has been stood on long enough to forget it was ever supposed to end.*

*I do not know if this is discovery or self-indulgence. I am aware these are not always different things.*

*I will return to this.*
