# Second Nature
## Crime, Law and Punishment in the Settling
### Part One

*Reading time: ~10 minutes*

---

She had chosen this. That was the first thing worth knowing about her, before any of the rest of it — not assigned, not fallen into out of necessity, not the only door that happened to open. She had tried other things first, the way most people tried other things first, drifting through the long open middle of a life that didn't require her to land anywhere in particular. She had spent two years on textiles. A year, restless, on nothing she could later describe coherently. And then, almost by accident, a neighbour's dispute over a shared wall and a misdirected drainage pipe, and she had found herself the person both sides wanted in the room, and had liked it more than she expected to, and had kept doing it until keeping doing it became simply what she did.

Nobody had made her a mediator. There was no licensing body, no examination, no badge. There was only the slow accumulation of people asking for her specifically, and the quiet satisfaction of being asked.

She thought about this sometimes, the strangeness of it. In the old accounts — and she had read a great many of them, more than was probably healthy for someone in her position — the people who did this work had been compelled into it by economics as much as by temperament. Lawyers because the debts demanded it. Mediators because the alternative was the courts, and the courts were slow and expensive and stacked against whoever had less. Even the good ones, the ones who genuinely cared about fairness, had been doing it inside a structure built on scarcity: somebody's time was money, somebody's case had a fee attached, somebody's freedom or property or future hung on an outcome that someone else had to be paid to argue for.

None of that applied to her. She did this because she wanted to, and she could stop wanting to whenever she liked, and the wanting had held steady for eleven years now, which she took as data.

---

The morning's dispute was, by any measure, small.

A woman — Tamsin, sixty-something, sharp-eyed, the kind of person whose composure was clearly costing her something — sat at one end of the table with her arms crossed. Across from her, a man named Joost, younger, perpetually apologetic in posture even when he wasn't apologizing in substance, sat with his hands flat on the wood as if bracing for something.

Between them, on the table, sat nothing. That was deliberate. She didn't bring evidence into the room the way the old courts had — exhibits, photographs, the theatre of proof. She had the facts already, verified, sitting quietly in the background where they belonged.

"The goat," Tamsin said, "has been in my seedlings three times this season."

"The fence held the first two times," Joost said. "I checked it myself after the second. It held."

"It held until it didn't."

The mediator let this sit for a moment before she spoke. This was most of the job, she had learned early — not the talking, but the precise calibration of when not to.

"The system has the record," she said, to both of them and neither. "Three incidents. The first two within the fence's rated tolerance for a goat of that weight pressing against it during the period in question — no fault attributable. The third: a section of fencing had degraded below tolerance, not yet visibly so, but measurably so if anyone had checked with instruments rather than eyes." She looked at Joost. "Nobody had checked with instruments."

"I check with my eyes," Joost said. "Everyone checks with their eyes."

"I know," she said. "I'm not assigning fault. I'm telling you what's known, so that the two of you can decide what to do with it."

This was the part of the work that the old accounts never quite seemed to anticipate, when she read them. They imagined, almost without exception, that once the facts were established, the rest followed mechanically — a verdict, a penalty, a closing of the file. They hadn't imagined a world in which establishing the facts was the easy part, handled cleanly and without dispute by something that didn't lie and didn't forget and had no stake in the outcome, and the hard part — the actual human part — started only after the facts were no longer in question.

Tamsin's seedlings were gone. That fact didn't change, whatever anyone decided. The garden bed could be replanted in a day, the new starts ready from the community nursery before the afternoon was out, no scarcity there, no waiting list, no cost that mattered to either of them. The goat would not be punished — what would that even mean, punishing a goat — and the fence would be repaired, properly this time, with sensors that would flag degradation before it reached failure. None of that was in dispute. None of that required her.

What required her was smaller and stranger and, she had come to believe, the actual point. Tamsin was not really angry about the seedlings. The seedlings had cost her nothing and would cost her nothing to replace. Tamsin was angry because she had said something, twice, and had not been listened to, and the third time had proven her right in a way that didn't feel like vindication so much as a confirmation of something she'd already suspected about not being taken seriously by her neighbour. That was the thing on the table, even though nobody had put it there directly. That was the thing the system couldn't verify or resolve, because it wasn't a fact. It was a feeling about facts, which was a different category of thing entirely, and the only tool that worked on it was time spent face to face, with someone in the room whose job was to notice when the real subject and the stated subject had quietly come apart.

"I did hear you," Joost said, eventually, not quite meeting her eyes. "The first time. I just — thought I'd fixed it. I wanted to have fixed it."

"That's not the same as fixing it."

"No," he agreed. "It isn't."

The mediator said nothing. This, too, was the job.

---

It took forty minutes, in the end. Forty minutes for an apology that meant something rather than the reflexive kind, for an agreement about sensors and a slightly wider buffer strip between the properties, for Tamsin's arms to uncross somewhere around the half-hour mark without either of them noticing it happen. Nobody was assigned a penalty. Nobody owed anybody anything in the sense that the word used to carry. The garden would be replanted. The fence would be better. The actual repair — the thing that mattered, the thing that would determine whether this happened again in some other form — was the forty minutes themselves, the fact of being heard by someone who had no other agenda in the room.

This was, she had come to think, most of what her work consisted of now. Not crime in the old sense — there was very little of that left to speak of, not because people had become better exactly, but because so much of what had driven it had simply stopped applying. Nobody stole out of hunger. Nobody defrauded because the alternative was losing a home or a livelihood; homes and livelihoods, in the old precarious sense, didn't exist to lose. The vast catalogue of offenses that had filled the old courts — theft, fraud, the long tail of crimes that were, when you looked closely enough, crimes of scarcity wearing different costumes — had thinned to almost nothing, the way a disease thins when you remove the conditions it needs to spread.

What was left was this. The human residue. Carelessness, pride, the slow accumulation of not-quite-saying-the-real-thing until it curdled into something sharper. Jealousy that had nothing to do with material want. Anger that arrived from somewhere nobody could quite locate. None of it required a courtroom. Most of it required exactly what had just happened in this room — a witness, a structure, enough patience to let the real subject surface.

She had never once, in eleven years, had to use a word like punishment. She had also never once had to pretend the work was always this small, this resolvable, this gentle in its outcome. She knew better than that. She had sat across from harder things, years ago, where forty minutes and an apology would have been an insult to what had actually happened, where the structure she relied on — verified facts, patient witness, the space for repair — had not been remotely equal to the size of the wound in the room. She didn't think about those cases often. She tried not to. But they didn't fully leave either, the way a scar doesn't leave, present and quiet and occasionally, for no obvious reason, making itself known.

---

Tamsin and Joost left together, which was its own small confirmation of what eleven years had taught her about endings that had actually ended. Not friends necessarily, not yet, but no longer at the particular kind of distance that had brought them to her table. She watched them go from the doorway, the morning still early enough that the light hadn't fully committed to the day, and felt the specific, undramatic satisfaction of something handled well.

She made tea. She sat for a while in the quiet that followed every session, a habit she'd never examined closely enough to call a ritual but that functioned like one.

And her mind did the thing it sometimes did, unbidden, in the gap after a case this small and this clean — drifted sideways to a case from years back that had never resolved the way this one just had, that hadn't resolved at all in any sense she was willing to call resolution. She didn't return to it often. She returned to it now only because the smallness of the morning's work had thrown its size into relief, the way a quiet room makes you notice a sound you'd otherwise have missed.

She had asked the system once, in the aftermath of that older case, whether it could have known. Not known what would happen — she understood by then that wasn't how any of this worked — but known something, some shape in the person beforehand, some pattern that might have given anyone a chance to stand differently in the path of what came next.

Its answer had stayed with her longer than she expected an answer to stay. It could see the shape a person carried, it told her. It would not act on a shape. Only on a deed.

She had turned that over many times since, in the quiet after easier sessions, and had never quite finished turning it over. She didn't expect to finish today either. She drank her tea, and let the morning be what it was, and did not reach for the rest of it.
