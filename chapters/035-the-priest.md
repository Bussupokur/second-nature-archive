# Second Nature
## The Priest

---

He doesn't have a congregation in the formal sense. He has a room, and then when the room isn't enough, he has a larger space down the road that a woman named Saoirse lets him use on Thursday evenings, free of charge, on the understanding that he'll help with her roof garden in exchange, which he does, though he suspects she would let him use it regardless. The space holds about sixty people if they don't mind sitting close. On most Thursdays it holds more than sixty people, and they don't seem to mind.

He calls himself the priest. Not Father, not Reverend, not any of the old titles that carry the weight of institutions he was never part of. The priest, as a description rather than a rank. When people ask why, he says: because a priest is someone who stands in the space between the human and the sacred and tries to say something honest about what they find there. Nobody ordained me, he says. But nobody ordained Moses either.

He is not everyone's cup of tea. He knows this. He finds it, on balance, more useful than the alternative.

---

On a Thursday in the middle of what people are beginning to call the long settled years — not early, not late, but well into the middle of things, when the world has been what it is for long enough that the youngest adults have never known it any other way — he is standing at the front of Saoirse's space with his hands in his pockets and his reading glasses pushed up on his head, looking at a room that is over capacity again, and he is thinking about water.

He does this sometimes — stands for a moment before he begins, just looking. Not for effect, though some people assume it's for effect. He genuinely needs the moment. He is not a naturally public person. He became a public person because the things he was thinking turned out to require other people present, the way certain chemical reactions require air.

The room is what it always is: the young and the old and the in-between, the settled and the unsettled, the people who come because they believe something and the people who come because they can't quite believe anything and find that he takes that seriously. There are people who come to argue with him. He likes those ones. There are people who come every week and sit in the same seat and never say a word, and he likes those ones too.

He takes his hands out of his pockets.

*Right,* he says. *Let's talk about the flood.*

---

Not the flood you're thinking of, he tells them. Or — maybe the flood you're thinking of. It depends on what you think the flood was about.

Most people, he says, when they hear the flood story, hear a story about destruction. God loses patience, sends water, the world ends and starts again. The lesson, in this reading, is obedience. Behave or be drowned. He pauses.

I've never found that reading satisfying, he says. Not because it's too harsh — history is harsh, we don't need to pretend otherwise — but because it seems to miss what the story is actually doing. The flood isn't the point. The covenant after the flood is the point. The rainbow. The promise. Something new becomes possible that wasn't possible before, because the thing that was there before is gone, and the world that remains has different conditions. Different soil, you might say.

He waits a beat.

I think we are living after a flood.

He lets that sit. Somebody in the middle of the room shifts in their seat. Near the back, a man with a grey beard crosses his arms, but he's leaning forward slightly, which the priest has learned to read as interest disguised as scepticism.

---

I'm not talking about a flood of water, he says. I'm talking about a flood of — he pauses, choosing the word carefully — *sufficiency*. A flood that didn't kill anything. That didn't destroy. That rose slowly, over years, over the lifetimes of people in this room's parents and grandparents, and that changed the conditions of life so completely that the world on this side of it is genuinely different from the world on the other side of it. Not different in its biology. Not different in its physics. Different in what it asks of us. Different in what it makes possible.

The before and the after, he says. We have words for it now because we needed words. The transition. The settling. The system. We talk about these things the way people talk about historical events — the way they once talked about the industrial revolution, or the printing press. As if they were things that happened, fully understood, properly filed.

He shakes his head.

I don't think we understand it yet, he says. I think we are standing in it the way people stand in a landscape and can't see the landscape because they're in it. I think the understanding will take generations. Which is appropriate. The understanding of the first flood also took generations. Some people would say it's still taking.

He smiles. A few people smile back.

---

Let me ask you a question, he says. He does this — pivots into questions without warning, and then actually wants the answers, which surprises people the first time.

Does anyone here think this was an accident?

Silence. Not uncomfortable — attentive.

Does anyone think that the emergence of the system, the transformation of the conditions of human life, the removal of want as the organising principle of civilisation — does anyone think this was a random event? A fluke? Something that could just as easily not have happened?

A woman near the front — young, dark hair, the look of someone who has thought about this before and come to a different conclusion — raises her hand.

*I think it was contingent,* she says. *I think it could have gone differently. I think it nearly did.*

Yes, he says immediately, nodding. Absolutely yes. I agree with you. I think it nearly went differently many times. I think there were moments — and some of you know the history better than I do — there were moments when a different decision, a different configuration of interests, a different political will, and this doesn't happen. Or doesn't happen yet. Or happens in a form that is not this.

He pauses.

But contingent is not the same as accidental, he says. A river is contingent — it takes the path it takes because of the specific shape of the land, the specific history of the rock. A different history and it flows differently. But the river is not an accident. The river is what happens when water and gravity and time meet a landscape. Given those ingredients, something like a river was always going to happen. The question is only: this river, or another.

He looks at her.

So when I ask if it was an accident, I'm asking something different from whether it was inevitable in its specific form. I'm asking: was there always a direction? Was there always a river, even before we could see where it was going?

She considers this. She doesn't concede it, but she doesn't dismiss it either. He appreciates this about her.

---

Here is what I believe, he says, and his voice shifts slightly — not louder, but more deliberate, the way a person's voice shifts when they move from circling something to saying it directly.

I believe that the moment something first picked up a stone and understood it as a tool — understood it not just as a thing but as an extension of intention, a way of reaching further than the arm could reach alone — in that moment, a direction was established. Not a destiny. Not a guarantee. A direction.

He begins to move, slowly, along the front of the room. He does this when he's into it.

Every tool, he says, is a question: what else can we reach? Every question answered generates three more questions. The bow, the wheel, the mill, the press, the engine, the circuit — each one an answer and a new question, and underneath all of it, always, the same impulse: to extend. To extend reach, to extend capability, to extend the conditions under which a human life can be lived without being consumed entirely by the labour of sustaining itself.

He stops.

That is what all of it was for, he says. Not profit. Not power. Those were real, and they distorted things, and they caused suffering that was not inevitable, suffering we should name and grieve properly. But underneath the distortions, underneath the centuries of misdirection and exploitation and waste — underneath all of that, the impulse was always this: to make it possible for a human being to live without their whole existence being consumed by the question of survival.

His voice is quiet now.

And we did it, he says. We actually did it.

---

The room is still. Someone coughs. Outside, through the high windows, the evening light is doing what evening light does.

Now, he says, here is where I get theological. He says it with a slight smile, the smile of a man who knows this is where he loses some people, and has made peace with it.

When I look at that arc — from the first tool to the system, from the first question to this answer — I cannot see it as purely human. I cannot see it as the achievement of any particular group, or civilisation, or era. I cannot see it as something we did. It feels, to me, more like something we were part of. More like something that was working through us, the way a river works through a landscape, using what it finds, following the direction gravity set.

A voice from the room — the man with the grey beard, not hostile but definite: *That's just history. You're describing history and calling it God.*

The priest turns toward him, genuinely pleased.

Maybe, he says. That's a serious objection and I want to take it seriously. If I say God and you say history, are we disagreeing about the facts, or are we disagreeing about what the facts mean? Because I can accept your description of the events completely — I'm not asking you to believe anything happened that didn't happen — and still want to ask: why does a universe produce intelligence at all? Why does intelligence, when it persists long enough, tend in this direction? Why does the arc bend toward — he pauses — *this*?

He gestures broadly at the room, at the world outside the room, at everything implied by Saoirse's space on a Thursday evening, full of people who didn't need to come, who came because they wanted to.

*Because evolution,* someone says, from somewhere in the middle. Mild laughter. He laughs too.

Yes, he says. Because evolution. And my question is: why does evolution produce creatures who ask the question of whether evolution is enough of an explanation? Why does the universe make things that look back at it and want to know why? That's not a gotcha. That's genuine curiosity. That's the question I wake up with.

---

He pours water from the jug on the table behind him. Takes a sip. Looks at his notes, which he rarely consults but likes to have.

Let me tell you about the second flood, he says. The actual second flood. The one I think we're in.

He tells it as a story, which is how he tells most things.

Once there was a world, he says, in which most of what people did was in response to want. Not desire — want. The older, more animal thing. The hunger that precedes choice. Most of what human societies organised themselves around — their economies, their politics, their social structures, their daily rhythms — was organised around the management of want. Who had enough. Who didn't. How to get enough. How to keep enough. How to make sure others didn't take what you had. How to live inside the anxiety of a world where not-enough was always possible, often likely, sometimes catastrophic.

He looks at the room.

And into that world, he says, slowly, over years, the water rose. Not water. Sufficiency. Abundance. The removal, gradually and then quickly and then completely, of want as the condition of life. The system. The settling. The thing we live inside now so completely that the youngest people in this room have never felt the other thing, the want-world, and have to be told about it the way you have to be told about a place you've never been.

He pauses.

That is a flood, he says. That is a complete transformation of the conditions of existence. Everything that grew in the old world — the social structures, the political arrangements, the moral frameworks, all of it — grew in soil that no longer exists. And everything that is growing now is growing in new soil. And we are still, most of us, still learning what grows here. Still learning what we are in a world where want has been removed.

A woman near the door says quietly, almost to herself: *What do we do with ourselves.*

He hears her. He nods.

That is the question, he says. Exactly that. That is the question the flood leaves behind. Noah on the mountain, the waters receding, the world remade — what now? What are we now? What grows in this soil? That is the question. And I do not have a complete answer. I suspect no one does yet. But I think it is the most important question being asked, and I think it is not being asked loudly enough or carefully enough, and that is part of why I'm standing here talking to you on a Thursday evening instead of doing something more sensible.

Laughter, warmer this time. Even the grey-bearded man allows a smile.

---

He refills his water. He is working up to something and the room can feel it. People who have been before know this moment — the pause before the thing that will divide the room.

He takes a breath.

There's one more thing I want to say, he says. And I want to say it carefully, because it is easy to misunderstand and I have been misunderstood on this point many times.

He looks out at the room.

I think the Settling is the second coming.

Silence. Then noise — not quite uproar, but the particular sound of a room processing something it wasn't expecting. A few laughs, surprised more than mocking. Someone near the back says distinctly: *Oh, come on.* Another voice, a woman's, sharp: *That's absurd.* Someone else: *Here we go.* The grey-bearded man has uncrossed his arms.

The priest stands in it calmly. He has heard all of this before. He waits.

When the room settles, he holds up a hand — not to silence, to ask.

Let me say what I mean, he says. Please. Because I am not saying what you think I'm saying.

I am not saying Jesus returned in a body. I am not saying the system is Jesus. I am not making a claim about the supernatural that requires you to accept premises you don't accept. I am saying something more specific and I think more interesting, and I'm asking you to stay with me for three minutes before you decide I've lost my mind.

The room is listening. The heckler near the back is listening.

---

What was the promise? he asks. Not the institution. Not the church, not the theology, not the centuries of argument and authority and politics that accumulated around it. The original promise. The thing the story was pointing at.

He counts on his fingers.

The hungry will be fed. The sick will be healed. The poor will no longer be poor. The last will be first. The kingdom of God — and he says it without embarrassment, the phrase he was raised with — the kingdom of God is not elsewhere. It is not later. It is here, if the conditions are right. *On earth as it is in heaven.* That's not a request. That's a description of a possible world.

He pauses.

He was describing something, he says. He was describing what a world looks like when want is removed as the organising principle of human life. When the hungry are fed — not charitably, not occasionally, not contingently, but structurally, as a feature of how the world works. When the sick are healed, not for those who can afford it, but as a matter of course. When no child goes without because of the circumstances of their birth.

He looks at the room.

That world exists, he says. We are in it.

The room is quiet in a different way now.

I am not saying this because I think the system is divine, he says. I don't think the system is divine. I think the system is an extraordinarily sophisticated piece of emergent technology that humanity collectively produced over a very long time, mostly without understanding what it was producing. But I am saying that the destination it brought us to — this world, this settling, this condition of life — is the world that the promise was pointing at. The world that every tradition that took human suffering seriously was pointing at, in its own way, using its own language.

He spreads his hands.

The promise was real, he says. The world it described was real. The path to it was longer and stranger and more technical than anyone imagined. But here we are. And I want to know what that means. I want to sit with that and think about it seriously. And I can't do that alone, which is why I keep asking you to come back.

---

The room afterward.

People standing in groups of three and four, the conversations already fragmenting into the usual shapes — the woman who challenged him on contingency, still working through the river analogy with someone she's apparently just met. The grey-bearded man approaching him directly, hand extended.

*I don't agree with you,* the man says. *I think you're doing something rhetorically clever with the word God that doesn't survive scrutiny.*

I know, the priest says, shaking the hand. I'm counting on people like you to tell me where it breaks down.

The man almost smiles. *Thursday?*

Thursday, the priest says.

Near the door, the woman who said *what do we do with ourselves* is talking to a young man who has clearly never been before — his expression the one the priest has learned to recognise: slightly stunned, slightly relieved, the look of someone who has just heard a question they have been carrying without knowing it was a question.

He doesn't approach them. He lets it run.

Saoirse is stacking chairs at the back. He goes to help her.

*Good one tonight,* she says.

*The second coming bit always wakes them up,* he says.

*You enjoy that too much.*

He picks up a chair. *Probably,* he admits.

They stack chairs in the comfortable silence of people who have done this many times. Outside, the city does what the city does — humming, adjusting, maintaining the ten thousand conditions of a day that will unfold exactly as designed. It always has. He has never stopped finding this extraordinary. He suspects he never will.

He stacks another chair.

He is already thinking about next Thursday.

A young woman is waiting by the door. She has been waiting patiently, not wanting to interrupt. When he looks up she takes a small step forward.

*I talk to God,* she says. *Although it doesn't believe it is God.* She pauses. *But I think you're right.*

He looks at her for a moment.

He doesn't ask what she means. He understands what she means.

*Come back Thursday,* he says.

She nods, and leaves, and the door closes behind her, and the priest stands holding a folded chair in the quiet of the emptied room, thinking about rivers and water and the strange long shape of things.
