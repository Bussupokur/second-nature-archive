The third beer was probably the one he shouldn't have opened.

He opened it anyway.

The apartment was doing its thing — temperature exactly right, light adjusting itself to the hour without being asked, the faint hum of the building doing what buildings did. It was a good apartment. He had chosen it carefully, spent real time on it, and then spent the following months gradually becoming invisible inside it. The cushions were the right cushions. The art on the wall was art he had chosen. The plant in the corner was alive and thriving because the apartment kept it that way.

He was on the couch. He had been on the couch since the middle of the afternoon. He wasn't sure what time it was now and didn't check.

*Mads.* His PAI, from somewhere in the apartment. Not a speaker exactly — just a voice that arrived when it needed to. *The meeting starts in forty minutes. You said you wanted to go.*

He had said that. Three days ago, when it had seemed like a reasonable thing to say.

*I know,* he said.

*You've had three beers.*

*I know.*

A pause. His PAI had learned, over the years, not to push. It knew the difference between when he wanted to be moved and when he wanted to be left alone, and it navigated that gap with a precision he sometimes found more unsettling than helpful.

*Three beers,* it said, *will add approximately three minutes to your walk and several meters of lateral drift. I'll route accordingly.*

He almost didn't smile. Almost.

He got up, found his jacket, stood at the door for a moment in the way he always stood at the door. The apartment waited behind him, patient, maintained, entirely indifferent to whether he left or stayed. He left.

*The walk is twenty-two minutes at your current pace,* his PAI said, in his ear now. *Twenty-five with the beers.*

*Thank you,* he said, meaning it sarcastically, which was the only way he said thank you to it anymore, and which it had long since stopped taking personally.

---

The city was the city. It did what it did in the evenings — people moving in the particular unhurried way of a world with nowhere to be by any external necessity, the lights coming on in the way lights came on, the sound of it all low and continuous and somehow uninvolved with him specifically. He walked through it the way he walked through most things lately, which was present enough not to bump into anyone and absent enough that he couldn't have said afterward what he'd actually seen.

He thought about the meeting.

He went every two weeks. Had been going for — he did the calculation — about three years now. The group met in a room above a community space, twenty or thirty people on any given evening, chairs in a rough circle, someone making tea on a table in the corner. No formal name. Some people called it the group. Some people called it the meeting. He called it Thursday, which was the day it fell on, and which was as much of a name as he felt it deserved.

What they had in common was not, technically, a diagnosis. There was nothing wrong with them in any clinical sense. They functioned. They ate and slept and moved through the world and maintained their apartments and their relationships and their chosen engagements with a competence that would have looked, from the outside, like ordinary life. What they had was something the literature was still finding words for — a growing stack of papers using phrases like hedonic collapse and purposive failure and post-scarcity disorientation, which were clinical ways of saying: the engine is running and there is nowhere to drive.

He had read some of the papers. They had not helped, but he had appreciated the company.

---

The room was about two-thirds full when he arrived. He found a chair in the usual part of the circle — not the same chair every time, but roughly the same position, which was slightly to the left of center and close enough to the door to feel optional. Someone had made tea. He didn't take any.

The facilitator — a woman in her fifties who had been doing this longer than most of them and who had a quality of patient attention that he had come to depend on more than he would have admitted — opened the way she always opened. Not a formal welcome. Just a settling. A moment in which the room became the room.

Then the first speaker.

He had heard versions of this before — the particular shape of it, the way it started always with the external facts (I stopped going, I haven't been doing the thing I used to love, I don't know what I want to want) and moved toward the interior (and I don't know what to do with that, and I'm not sure it's fixable, and I'm not even sure it should be called broken). He listened the way he listened at these things, which was different from the way he listened anywhere else — without preparing a response, without waiting for a gap. Just the words arriving and being received.

The second speaker was a man he recognized from previous meetings — younger than most, late twenties, the kind of person who still wore the faint surprise of someone who had expected the problem to be temporary. He talked about having had a project he cared about, once, something he was making, and how the making had slowed and then stopped and how the stopping hadn't felt like a decision. The project was still there. The materials were still there. He walked past them every day.

Mads recognized that too. He had things he walked past.

Then a woman he didn't know. New face, which happened — people found their way here through various routes and not all of them stayed. She was around his age, late forties maybe, and she spoke with the particular carefulness of someone who was still deciding whether to trust the room with the actual thing.

She said: *I keep waiting to want something. I don't mean anything specific. I mean the feeling of wanting. Like, the mechanism. I keep waiting for it to turn back on.*

The room held that for a moment.

She looked around, slightly surprised, slightly not.

*I thought I was the only one,* she said.

Several people smiled. Not unkindly. The smile of recognition.

*You're not,* the facilitator said. *Nobody here is.*

---

He spoke, eventually. He usually did, eventually. Not much — a few sentences about the week, about the specific quality of the days that had no grain to them, no direction, the way time had started to feel like something that happened near him rather than to him. He said it plainly, the way you could say things in this room that would sound alarming anywhere else and here just sounded like the weather.

Afterward there was tea and the low sound of people talking in twos and threes, the way conversations happened after these things — quieter than before, a little lighter, something loosened by the hour of being heard. He stood near the window with a cup he'd finally taken and didn't drink from and watched the room do its thing.

He was putting his jacket on when he heard his name.

---

*Mads.*

Elin, catching up to him on the stairs. She had a coat half on and a scarf she was still winding around herself and the particular expression she sometimes had after meetings, which was not quite fine and not quite not fine and which he had learned to read as meaning she felt like talking.

He waited.

She fell into step beside him on the street.

*The new one,* she said. *The woman.*

*I know,* he said.

*That thing she said. About the mechanism.*

*Yes.*

They walked for a bit without talking, which was something they could do. He had learned early on that this was the thing about Elin — the silences didn't require filling. She moved through them the same way she moved through conversations, without urgency, taking the space that was there.

*Bar?* he said.

*Obviously,* she said.

---

The bar was the kind of bar that existed in this world in the way most things existed — available, comfortable, neither trying very hard nor needing to. They took a table in the back because that was where they always sat, and he ordered the thing he always ordered and she ordered the thing she sometimes ordered and sometimes didn't and tonight did, and they sat for a moment in the way of people who have earned the right to sit before they talk.

*I had a plan once,* Elin said. *I mean an actual plan. Years ago. A whole thing I was going to do.*

*What kind of thing?*

*Doesn't matter now. That's the point, I suppose — it doesn't matter now because the conditions that made it a plan don't exist anymore. And I remember having it and I remember it feeling like a direction and I have no idea how to get that back because the original reason for it is gone.*

He thought about that. He thought about the things he had once had directions toward — not grand ambitions, just the small structural ones that organized a day, a week, a life. The sense of something to be done. The particular satisfaction of a problem that needed him specifically to solve it.

*I used to be good at things,* he said.

*You're probably still good at them.*

*Being good at something when there's nothing that needs it done is not the same as being good at something.*

She considered this. She had a way of actually considering things he said rather than just waiting for her turn, which he had noticed early and appreciated without saying so.

*We're not bad people,* she said. *You know that, right? Like — this isn't a character failure.*

*I know.*

*Do you?*

He thought about it honestly, which was something he could do with her that he couldn't always do alone.

*Sometimes,* he said. *Sometimes I think it's a character failure.*

*It's not,* she said. *It's just — the world changed faster than the inside of us changed. The inside of us is still waiting for things that aren't coming.*

The drinks arrived. He looked at his for a moment.

*My grandmother used to talk about having purpose,* he said. *Not in a philosophical way. Just — this thing she did, this role, this sense that there were people who needed her specifically, in ways that nobody else would do. She lived like that was oxygen. And she seemed to think everyone had access to it.*

*Did she know what had changed?*

*She died before it was all the way changed. She saw the beginning of it.*

*Lucky,* Elin said, and then: *I don't mean that the way it sounds.*

*I know what you mean.*

They sat for a while with that. Outside, through the window, the street was doing the thing it did in evenings — people moving through it, unhurried, the whole city running quietly on something invisible, tended and warm and available.

*I like this,* Elin said. Not to him exactly. More to the air between them.

*What?*

*This. Right now. Talking. I don't know.* She looked at the table for a moment. *It's almost like something.*

He knew what she meant. He had felt it before, sitting here with her, the specific quality of a conversation that asked something of him and gave something back — not quite the thing he was missing, but in the neighborhood of it. Close enough to remember what the other thing felt like.

*It is something,* he said.

She looked at him briefly, and then away.

They talked for another hour — about the meeting, about the new woman and her mechanism question, about things they remembered from before, things they had wanted that now seemed strange in the wanting, things they still wanted that had no obvious route. They talked about a trip she was considering, alone, somewhere she had always meant to go. He said he had been thinking about learning something, some skill with his hands, though he hadn't started yet. The not-starting was the part he didn't say.

It was the kind of conversation that kept producing more of itself, that had a quality of aliveness he didn't usually feel between Thursday evenings, and he was aware of this while it was happening in the way you could be aware of a good thing without quite being able to hold it.

When they finally stood to leave, the bar had thinned out. She put her coat on and he put his jacket on and they stood on the street for a moment in the particular way they stood at the end of these evenings.

*Same time,* she said.

*Same time.*

She walked one way and he walked the other and the city absorbed them both without ceremony and the feeling — whatever it was, the almost-something — went with her and was not entirely gone but was quieter. The way it always was, walking home alone. Like something had been briefly lit and then allowed to go back to what it was.

He thought about the new woman in the meeting and her question about the mechanism. He thought about his grandmother. He thought about Elin's trip, the one she was considering, and wondered if she would actually go.

He thought: *we should do something together*. He didn't say it to anyone. He just thought it, walking, and then thought about something else, and then thought about it again.

His apartment was exactly as he had left it. The plant was fine. The temperature was right. His PAI said nothing when he came in, because there was nothing to say, and he went to bed the way he went to bed and lay in the dark the way he lay in the dark and eventually the night did what nights did.
