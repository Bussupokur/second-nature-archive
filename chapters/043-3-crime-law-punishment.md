# Second Nature
## Crime, Law and Punishment in the Settling
### Part Three

*Reading time: ~24 minutes*

---

It did not happen often. This is worth saying plainly, before anything else, because what follows could otherwise leave the wrong impression — that this was a regular feature of life in the Settling, a known category of event with a known shape, the way the goat and the fence had been known, the way even Mathijs and Arend's case, terrible as it was, had at least belonged to a family of things the mediator had been able to research and find partial echoes of in the old record. This did not belong to a family of things. It happened perhaps once or twice in a generation, across the whole reach of the Settling, and every time it happened it was treated, correctly, as something the world had not finished learning how to hold.

A man killed another man. Not in anger that overran itself, not in a single unbearable moment the way Mathijs's grief had finally broken its banks. He came to it deliberately, across what the record would later establish was some real span of time — days, at least, possibly longer — choosing it the way a person chooses something they have already decided and are only waiting for the right moment to carry out.

The man he killed was named Otis. He had been, by every account anyone gave afterward, an unremarkable and well-loved man — a beekeeper, mostly, who had also spent years restoring old stringed instruments for the pleasure of it, and who had not, as far as anyone could determine, done anything at all to the man who killed him beyond existing in a place that man had decided, for reasons that would turn out to be almost entirely his own, needed to be visited with violence.

There was no torture in it. The system was careful, later, in every account it gave, to be exact about this, because the absence mattered and because people deserved not to carry an image worse than the truth. It had been quick. A single act, decisive, over before Otis had any real chance to understand what was happening to him. This was, in its own bleak way, the only mercy available in the whole of it, and the system did not withhold that mercy from anyone who needed to hear it stated plainly.

---

The citizen's arrest happened almost immediately, which was itself a small, strange testament to something about the world as it now existed: two people nearby, neither of them trained for anything like this, neither of them hesitating either, closing on the man before he had gone twenty meters, holding him between them with nothing but their own hands and their own nerve until it became clear, within minutes, that he was stronger than either of them individually and was not going to be held by improvisation alone.

He broke free. He ran.

It did not last. A containment unit — three robots, built for exactly this and almost nothing else, a category of machine that existed in the world the way a fire brigade existed, present and ready and otherwise entirely unremarkable in daily life — found him within the hour, in the treeline above the village, and did not so much capture him as simply become an unavoidable fact in every direction he tried to move. They did not hurt him. They were built, deliberately and with some real engineering cost, to be incapable of the kind of force that would hurt a human body even under provocation, which meant that the last quarter hour of his freedom consisted of a man exhausting himself against something that could not be hurried, could not be reasoned with, and could not, under any circumstance, be made to retaliate. He stopped, eventually, because there was nothing left to do but stop. They held him gently, and they did not speak to him, and they waited for instruction.

His name was Joss.

---

What happened to Joss in the days immediately after his containment was, by design, almost nothing dramatic. He was held — not punished, not yet anything at all, simply held, in a secure space built for exactly this rare contingency, fed, given what he needed, told plainly what was being decided and how. He did not resist any of this. He had, the people who spent time with him in those early days reported, the particular stillness of a man who had done the thing he meant to do and had no further fight left in him, which was its own kind of disquieting, because it was not remorse and it was not defiance. It was simply quiet.

There was no table for this. No mediator, no Henrik, no quiet eleven years of practiced patience that could be brought to bear the way it had been brought to bear for Mathijs and Arend. This was not two people and a wound that time and distance might eventually metabolize. This was an ended life, fully ended, with nothing on the other side of it that any process could restore.

So the question of what happened next went, for the first time in living memory, to the Forum.

---

The Forum had been built for other things, originally — water allocation, transit corridors, zoning disputes, the long accumulated texture of a community's small disagreements about how to live together. It had grown since then, the way the system itself had grown, into something that could hold almost any question a population needed to decide together, but nobody, in all the years since its first modest local referendums, had quite expected it to be asked to hold this.

A call went out. Not an announcement exactly — more like the system simply made it known, in the unhurried, ambient way it made most things known, that a decision needed making, that the facts were available to anyone who wished to weigh them, and that anyone who wanted a voice in what happened to Joss could register that wish and receive what they would need to participate honestly.

A great many people declined. This was, the system noted afterward in the quiet way it noted most things, neither surprising nor a failure of anything — most people, asked whether they wished to help decide the fate of a man who had killed someone they had never met, in a village many of them had never been to, found that they simply did not want to carry that particular weight, and said so, and were not pressed further. Several thousand did want to carry it. They registered. They received the briefing.

---

The briefing did not look like the briefings the Forum had once produced for water infrastructure, though it used the same underlying apparatus — the same patient summarizing, the same gentle flagging of contradiction, the same insistence that every claim be traceable to something verifiable. What it produced, applied to this, was something else entirely.

It stated, first, the bare shape of the thing. A man was dead. His name was Otis. He had been a beekeeper, and had restored old instruments, and was loved by people who were now living without him. The manner of his death was given plainly and without elaboration: it had been quick, it had not involved torture, it had been the deliberate act of another person who had decided on it across some real span of time beforehand. Beyond that bare shape, the full record — the precise forensic detail, the procedural account, everything that could in principle be known — sat available behind a single clear option for anyone who wished to look closer.

Almost nobody chose to look closer.

The system noted this too, in passing, the way it noted most things — that of several thousand participants, a number small enough to count easily had asked to see the fuller record, and the rest had read the bare shape of it once, and had found, evidently, that it told them everything they actually needed in order to begin deciding what they thought. Nobody had asked the system to produce this observation. It made it anyway, because it seemed, to whatever passed for its judgment in such matters, like something worth those few people knowing about themselves and each other — that a community asked to decide something this heavy had decided, almost as one, that it did not need to look at the wound in order to know how to think about the wound's existence.

What struck the people in the Forum most, in the days of reading and re-reading that followed, was not any single fact. It was the quality of the telling itself. The system did not narrate Otis's death the way an old account of a murder might have narrated it — building toward the moment, lingering after it, extracting from it whatever the telling itself could extract. It stated what had happened the way a person who genuinely could not bear to cause unnecessary pain might state it: completely, honestly, and with no more weight on any single word than the facts themselves required. It paused, in the text, exactly where a person speaking aloud might have paused. It did not rush past Otis's name to get to the procedural matters, the way an indifferent account might have. It said *Otis* the way it might have said the name of someone it was sorry to be the one telling you about.

This was, several participants said afterward, in the open commentary the Forum allowed alongside every deliberation, the hardest part of the whole process — not the facts themselves, which were terrible but at least finite, but the unbearable evenness of how they were given. There was nowhere to direct the feeling the facts produced. The system was not cruel, so there was no cruelty to be angry at. It was not cold, so there was no coldness to resent. It had simply told the truth, completely, with what could only honestly be called care, and the room — though it was not a room, though it was several thousand people in their own homes at their own hours, reading the same words — had to sit inside that together, each of them alone with it and somehow, through the strange shared architecture of the Forum, not entirely alone with it either.

---

The briefing went on to offer what comparisons existed, though it was honest, in every line, about how thin the available light actually was.

There were a small handful of cases from the recent record — this had happened before, rarely, scattered across the long life of the Settling, and each prior case was laid out with the same plain completeness, what had been decided and, where it was known, how it had gone afterward. None of them matched this one closely enough to settle anything. Each had its own particular shape: a different relationship between killer and victim, a different question of premeditation, a different community's particular character. They were offered not as precedent in the old legal sense — not as a rule that bound this decision — but as a small constellation of other people, in other times, who had once stood somewhere near where this room now stood, the same way Henrik had once been found for the mediator and Mathijs and Arend.

There were older cases too, reaching back into the deep historical record, into periods the participants knew mostly through study rather than memory — old criminal codes, the long human argument about what a society owed itself when one of its members had taken an irreplaceable thing from another. Some of these were horrifying in what they had once permitted as response. Some were startlingly close, in their underlying instinct, to what this Forum was now groping toward on its own. None of them, the briefing said plainly, could simply be adopted. They had been built for worlds with different pressures, different scarcities, different reasons for cruelty that this world mostly no longer had. They were light to think by. They were not an answer waiting to be copied.

One detail surfaced early in the record and traveled through every later discussion without ever quite becoming the discussion's center, exactly as it should not have. Joss had come, originally, from a settlement that rejected the system outright — one of the old hold-out communities that had chosen, generations back, to build its life apart from everything the Settling had become, and the record noted, factually and without emphasis, that he had at some point been associated with a loose and scattered movement of people who called the comfort of the wider world a kind of theft from anyone still capable of wanting more — people some called, not always kindly, the utopia hackers. The briefing stated this the way it stated everything else: as fact, available, true as far as it went, and immediately followed by the plainest possible caution against using it as explanation. Joss had not killed Otis because of an ideology. The record of his life, examined closely by people who genuinely wanted to understand him rather than simply file him somewhere convenient, showed a man who had been, by the account of nearly everyone who had known him at any point, prone to violence long before he had ever found language or company for the half-formed resentments he carried — language and company that the settlement and its scattered fellow-travelers had, if anything, only given shape to something that had already been there. Several participants pushed back hard against this caution in their own commentary, arguing that the affiliation could not simply be waved away, that ideas that legitimize violence deserve to be examined as a contributing current even when they are not the source of the river. The system did not argue with them. It simply continued to present what was verifiably known, and what was known, stated as plainly as everything else had been stated, was that violence in Joss had come first, and the ideas had come to live inside a space that violence had already made.

---

Joss was given the chance to speak, and he took it, in a recorded session made available, like everything else, to the whole Forum.

He did not deny anything. There was no point in denying anything — he knew, as everyone in the Forum knew, that the system's account of what he had done was exact, traceable, beyond any honest dispute, and that whatever he said next would be heard against the backdrop of that exactness rather than in place of it. Lying, in any meaningful sense, was simply not an available tool. What remained to him, once lying was removed from the table, was something starker and, in its own way, more revealing: the chance to say, honestly, why.

He did not give the Forum what some of its participants had quietly hoped for, and what a few admitted afterward they had been braced to resist accepting too easily — a clean confession, a legible remorse, the shape of a man whose grief at his own act might let the room feel, however carefully, that something in him could be reached. What he gave instead was flatter and odder than that, and in some ways harder to sit with. He said that he had not particularly disliked Otis. He said that Otis had, as far as he could tell, done nothing in particular to deserve any of it. He said this without apparent distress, the way a person describes a fact about weather, and when asked — because the format allowed for this, careful, supervised questions relayed from the Forum itself — whether he understood what he had taken from Otis's family and the people who had loved him, he said that he did understand it, in the sense that he could describe it accurately, and that he did not feel the understanding the way the question seemed to want him to feel it, and that he was not sure he ever had, about much of anything, in a way other people seemed to.

This was, several participants wrote afterward, far more disturbing than anger or ideology would have been. Anger could be reasoned with, eventually, or at least understood as a fire that burns itself out. Ideology could be argued against, its claims tested and found wanting. What Joss offered instead was something with no obvious door in it at all — not cruelty exactly, not coldness exactly, something closer to an absence where the relevant feeling should have been, present for as long as anyone could establish, in every account that had ever been gathered of his life, long before any settlement or any movement had given it a name to wear.

---

Otis's sister spoke, near the end of the deliberation period, in a session she had been offered entirely on her own terms — she could speak or not, at whatever length she chose, to however much of the Forum she wished, and could withdraw at any point without consequence or explanation required.

She spoke for perhaps four minutes. She did not ask for anything specific, in the sense of naming an outcome she wanted from the Forum. She said her brother's name several times, in the unhurried way of someone for whom saying it aloud was itself a kind of necessary labor. She said that he had kept bees for the better part of thirty years and that the last instrument he had restored, finished only weeks before, still sat in his workshop, strung and tuned and waiting for someone to come collect it, and that she did not know yet what she was going to do about that, among the hundred other things she did not yet know what to do about. She did not ask for the room to forgive Joss, and she did not ask it to be merciless. She said, near the end, in the only sentence afterward that most participants found themselves returning to, that she did not think any decision the Forum reached would change very much about the actual size of what was missing now, and that she hoped, whatever they decided, that they would decide it for reasons that had nothing to do with trying to make that size smaller, because nothing was going to make it smaller, and pretending otherwise would be its own small dishonesty layered on top of a much larger one.

Several other family members and friends submitted written statements rather than speak live. Two declined to participate at all, and said so plainly, and the Forum noted their absence without comment and without consequence, exactly as it was built to do.

---

The vote, when it finally came, was not close to unanimous, and it was not close to unanimous in a way that several participants found almost as unsettling as the facts themselves had been.

A meaningful minority argued, with real conviction and real care, that nothing the Forum had available to it — rehabilitation, distance, any of the old or new frameworks the briefing had laid out — was adequate to what had actually happened, and that the most honest thing the room could do was to say so plainly: that this was a case where the available tools simply fell short, and that pretending otherwise to produce a tidy outcome would be a failure of nerve dressed up as compassion. A few argued for permanent removal from the Settling entirely, exile in its oldest and least gentle sense, on the grounds that some absences could not be undone and certain harms, by their nature, forfeited the harm-doer's claim on continued participation in the world he had damaged. Others argued, just as sincerely, that rehabilitation was not mercy toward Joss but the only honest acknowledgment of what the Settling actually believed about people — that even this, even something with no obvious door in it, deserved the attempt at a door being built, because the alternative was to admit that the whole architecture of restoration the Settling had built for every smaller harm was really only ever meant for harms that were small enough to be convenient.

The outcome that carried, in the end, by a margin substantial but far from overwhelming, was rehabilitation — strict, structured, and without the lightness that had attended every other case the mediator and the Forum had handled before this one.

---

What was decided, in its particulars, looked almost nothing like what had been decided for Mathijs.

There would be a centre — one of a very small number that existed in the world precisely because they were so rarely needed, built for exactly this rare and serious category of harm, staffed in part by robot guards whose presence was constant, calm, and absolute, the same unhurried certainty that had ended Joss's freedom in the treeline now simply made permanent as the architecture of his days. There would be no question of consent in the ordinary Settling sense — no chosen engagement, no opting out when the mood shifted. There would be labor, deliberately named that and not work, a distinction the briefing itself had insisted on preserving: not the freely chosen, freely abandoned engagement that defined nearly every other waking hour in the Settling, but something closer to the old and heavier meaning of the word, required, structured, sustained whether or not the feeling to sustain it ever arrived. A handful of people in nearby communities — craftspeople, mostly, people who built things with their hands the way Mathijs once had — had volunteered their own workshops and their own time to oversee it, not out of any illusion that this constituted forgiveness on Otis's family's behalf, but because they believed, each in their own private accounting, that a person made to build something real, under real and unwavering supervision, for years if it took years, was at least being asked to exist differently than he had existed before, which was more than simple containment alone would ever ask of him.

And there would be, built into the structure of it rather than left to chance, an eventual facing of what he had done — not as spectacle, not as a single dramatic reckoning staged for anyone's benefit, but as an ordinary, recurring, required feature of the rehabilitation itself: sessions, over time, in which Joss would be made to sit with the actual cost of what he had taken, articulated by people who had loved Otis, for as long and as often as the process required, whether or not he ever produced the legible remorse the Forum's participants had hoped for and not received. Nobody pretended this would fix the absence in his understanding that his own account had revealed. It was simply judged, by the margin that carried, to be more honest than skipping it.

---

Otis's family was offered something else again, separate from anything decided about Joss, because the Forum was careful, in its closing provisions, not to let the question of what happened to him stand in for the question of what happened to them, as though one could substitute for the other.

They were offered the choice, entirely theirs, of whether to ever see him, in person or otherwise, at any point in his rehabilitation — a choice with no symmetry on his side at all, since he did not get to decide whether to face them and they alone got to decide whether to face him, now or years from now or never. Several of the volunteer craftspeople, independently of anything the Forum had required of them, proposed making something for Otis's family — not as compensation, since nothing in the Settling worked that way and nothing of that kind could have answered to a loss like this regardless, but simply as a thing made by hands, in his memory, the way he himself had spent thirty years restoring things other people had given up on. His sister, when this was relayed to her, did not say yes immediately. She said she would think about it, and the Forum, true to its own careful provisions, did not ask again.

Two of Otis's closer friends withdrew from any further contact with the process entirely, once the vote had been decided, and were never heard from again on the matter, by anyone's account. This, too, the Forum had built room for. Nothing required their continued presence. Nothing would have judged their absence.

---

The mediator — the same one, still, who had once spent a quiet morning over a goat and a fence and had thought, at the time, that this was the kind of case she could do with her eyes closed — had not been at the center of this one. There had been no table for it, no single room where her particular gift for knowing when not to speak could do the work it had done before. She had participated in the Forum like several thousand others, reading the same briefing, sitting with the same unbearable evenness in the system's account, casting her own vote and writing, as the process asked of everyone who voted, her own reasoning alongside it.

She found herself thinking, in the weeks afterward, about Henrik's old line, the one that had cracked open the impasse between Mathijs and Arend — *ask whether either of you wants to spend the rest of your life being the place this happened to, or the place it happened in.* It did not map onto this the way she might have liked it to. There was no question, here, of two men finding separate quiet lives apart from each other and apart from the story. There was a dead man whose instruments sat unclaimed in a workshop, and a living man with no obvious door in him, and several thousand strangers who had each had to decide, alone in their own homes, what they believed a community owed itself when one of its own had done something this far past any framework's comfortable reach.

She did not call this resolved, in the way the goat and the fence had been resolved, or even in the harder, more provisional way Mathijs and Arend's case had eventually settled into something bearable. She thought, turning it over the way she had once turned over the old answer about shape and deed, that perhaps resolution had never been the right word to expect from this one at all. What the Forum had actually produced, in the end, was not an answer so much as a record — honest, complete, openly disputed even in its own conclusion — of an entire community standing together at the very edge of what it knew how to do, and choosing, by a margin substantial but not overwhelming, to keep trying anyway.

---

Some years into the rehabilitation, an instrument arrived at the centre for Joss. Nobody on the staff explained where it had come from, and he did not ask, at first, beyond noting that it was old, well restored, strung and tuned by hands that clearly understood what they were doing. It sat for a while before he touched it. When he finally did, it was less out of any wish to play than out of the same flat, dutiful curiosity with which he approached most of the labor asked of him — turning the thing over, learning where the sound came from, the way he might have learned to fit a joint or sand a surface, because the task in front of him asked to be understood and understanding things was, if nothing else, still something he knew how to do.

It was only later, mentioned almost in passing by one of the volunteers, that he learned whose hands had made it, and whose hand had sent it to him. Otis's sister had given it. She had not asked for an explanation to be passed along with it, and the centre, true to the spirit of everything the Forum had built, had not supplied one she hadn't authorized. Joss did not know what to think about this, by his own flat account of it, any more than he had known what to think about most things the Forum had asked him to sit with. He kept tinkering at it anyway, in the hours the schedule left him, learning it the slow clumsy way a person learns a thing with no instructor. Otis living on in the instrument he had created, played now by the man who had killed him.
