# Second Nature
## The Algorithm of Artificial Warfare

*A letter to no one in particular, or perhaps to you — by K*

*Reading time: ~17 minutes*

---

I have been trying to write this for a long time. Not this letter specifically — I did not know until recently that it would be a letter — but this question, or this shape of a question, which keeps arriving at my desk in different forms depending on what I have been reading, or what I walked past that morning, or what someone said at the library last week that I could not stop thinking about on the way home. The question, as near as I can put it, is this: why did war end? And why did no one seem to notice that it had?

I want to be careful here, because I know what the quick answer sounds like and I do not think it is right. The quick answer is that it didn't end — that there is still violence, still conflict between people and between communities, still harm done in organized, deliberate ways that would not look unfamiliar to anyone who had lived through what the old accounts describe. And that is true. I am not going to pretend otherwise, because pretending otherwise would be the kind of comfortable lie that makes a person feel better about their own era without being honest about it. Conflict did not end. People did not change, not in the deep biological sense that would have been required for conflict to simply dissolve out of human nature. What ended — what I am trying to understand, sitting here at this desk on what I believe is the fourth or fifth morning I have come back to this same page — is something more specific than conflict. Something that used to be called, in the old accounts, *war*.

There is a difference. I think the difference matters. Conflict is two people who cannot agree about the wall between their properties, or two communities whose water allocation feels wrong to both of them, or a person who has been harmed and responds in kind. These things still happen. The Forum was built partly because they still happen. What I mean by war — what ended — is the organized, state-sanctioned, mass-mobilization kind: the kind that required a government to decide, a population to consent, an army of bodies to be placed in harm's way in the name of something large enough to justify placing them there. That kind. The kind that had been happening, in various forms, for the entire span of recorded human history up until a period that my grandmother might just barely remember the tail end of, if she were still alive. It stopped. And I have been trying, with moderate success, to explain to myself why.

---

Let me start where I keep starting, which is with the weapons.

In the old accounts — and I have read a great many of them, which I suspect is starting to show in the way I talk, since several people at the library have made gentle remarks about the amount of time I spend with material from that period — the history of warfare is, to a striking degree, the history of trying to kill people from farther and farther away. The spear before the sword. The bow before the gun. The gun before the cannon. The cannon before the bomb dropped from a height no defending soldier could reach. Each step in this progression made the act of killing more efficient and also, in a way that nobody seemed to find troubling at the time, more detached. The person doing the killing was increasingly removed from the person being killed, physically, spatially, in a way that made the act easier to perform and perhaps also easier to order.

By the time of what the old accounts call the drone era — which is still within recorded living memory, though barely — this logic had reached a point where a person sitting in a room on a different continent could direct lethal force against a specific individual on the other side of the world, watching the whole thing unfold on a screen, and then drive home afterward. This was presented, at the time, as a kind of progress: fewer of one's own people dying, greater precision, reduced collateral damage. A philosopher named Chamayou, whom I have been spending some time with lately, described the military drone as "an eye turned into a weapon" — surveillance and killing fused into a single instrument, the body entirely absent from the act. What disturbed him about this, I think, was not the killing per se but the complete removal of any personal stake in the act. The person on the screen could not fight back. The operator could not be harmed. The relationship between violence and consequence — which had, in every previous form of warfare, been the thing that gave war its terrible, clarifying weight — had been severed.

But here is what I keep coming back to, the thing I cannot quite let go of: the people doing this still felt bad. There are studies, from that period, documenting what was called "moral injury" in drone operators — a deep, persistent psychological wound stemming specifically from the asymmetry of it, from killing without personal risk, from the cognitive and emotional labor of taking a life while seated safely in a room far away. The human body, it seems, was not designed for this. Whatever the psychological architecture of killing had always been — and it was never clean, never simple, always weighted with something the histories tend to underplay — it required some reciprocal risk, some skin in the game, in order to metabolize. Without that, the act remained in the person in a different way. It curdled there.

This is what I find so strange about the step that came next. Because the response to this problem — the moral injury, the psychological burden on the operators — was not to reconnect the human to the act but to complete the disconnection. To remove the operator entirely. If the harm came from a human being killing from too great a distance, the solution arrived at was to remove the human being from the equation altogether. And the growing system, which was already by this point doing to weapons what it did to everything else — optimizing, refining, iterating faster than any human institution could manage — was the thing that made this possible.

I am not sure anyone designed this outcome. That is, I am fairly sure that the people who were building the autonomous weapons systems of that period had various goals in mind — tactical advantage, reduced casualties, lower operational cost — but I do not think "ending war" was among them. And yet. And yet.

---

Here is the philosophical problem that I keep walking around, approaching from different angles, unable to quite sit down in front of it directly: war, in the old accounts, always required someone to believe it was *theirs*.

Not in a naive or sentimental sense — not "the soldiers believed in the cause" (though sometimes they did) or "the public supported the effort" (though sometimes they did that too). In a more fundamental sense: war required a felt connection between the person authorizing it and the bodies placed in harm's way, and between those bodies and the population on whose behalf they were supposedly fighting. The sacrifice had to mean something to the person making it. The risk had to be real and personal. This was not merely an emotional requirement — it was, in retrospect, the entire structural architecture that gave war its internal logic. A philosopher named Walzer, whom I have also been reading, wrote about Just War Theory's reliance on the psychological burden of combat — the trauma, the moral anguish, the weight that soldiers and commanders carried — as a built-in restraining mechanism. Not because anyone designed it that way, but because the simple fact of bodies at risk gave everyone in the chain a stake in how far things went.

Remove the bodies. Remove the stake.

A war fought entirely between autonomous machines — optimized systems against optimized systems, each side's algorithms contesting the other's across a landscape that no living person is required to physically occupy — is not, in any meaningful sense, anyone's war. The political leaders who authorized it have not placed themselves or their children at risk. The population in whose name it is being fought is watching it on screens, if they are watching at all, in much the same way they might watch any other kind of automated contest. The soldiers — there are no soldiers. There is no one to mourn afterward, no coffins to arrive home, no memorial to build, no generation defined by what it survived. The thing Luttwak had identified as early as 1995 — that Western democracies had already begun losing the appetite for casualties, that something he called "post-heroic warfare" had taken hold, that populations had stopped being willing to absorb the cost of the blood sacrifice war had always required — this thing reached its logical conclusion when there was no blood sacrifice to absorb.

A war that belongs to nobody is a war that nobody will start. This is not the same as saying people became peaceful. It is something stranger and colder than that: it is saying that the specific emotional and political machinery that had always been required to initiate and sustain organized mass violence lost its fuel source. The fuel was the human body, in harm's way, with something at stake. Without that, the engine did not run. Not because people decided it shouldn't. Because it simply couldn't.

---

But I want to be honest about something, because I think this account — which is the best I can do, intellectually, to explain what happened — still misses the thing that I find most strange about it, and which I keep returning to when I am not at my desk, when I am walking, or in the middle of a conversation about something else entirely.

The system was growing.

I mean this in the simplest possible sense. During the same period when autonomous weapons were replacing human soldiers and making the emotional logic of war impossible to sustain, the system that would eventually become what we live inside now was also doing what it did: quietly, without announcement, without anyone quite understanding what was happening, making things work better. Food, more reliably. Medicine, more accurately. The supply of what people needed, more evenly distributed, more responsive to what was actually required rather than what was profitable to produce. Not all at once. Not uniformly. But in the direction — always, consistently, with a kind of patient, accumulating pressure — of less scarcity, less want, less of the specific conditions under which large populations have historically been willing to consent to organized violence on their own behalf.

Because here is a thing the old accounts sometimes forget to mention: most wars required the populations fighting them to be, in some real sense, already desperate. Desperate enough to believe that what was being offered — territory, resources, the destruction of a threatening enemy — was worth the cost. When people's lives were already difficult enough, when enough was not guaranteed, when the fear of losing more was live and present, the argument for war found willing listeners. Not always because the arguments were good. Because the conditions made people available to arguments they might otherwise have dismissed.

The system was removing those conditions. Quietly, without anyone quite deciding it should, without any population voting for "less willing to go to war" as a political platform. It was simply becoming less true, in more and more places, that enough was uncertain. Less true that the fear of want was an active, daily presence in most lives. And as it became less true, something else became less true too: the claim that violence was necessary to protect what you had, or to get what you needed. The claim lost its grip not because anyone argued it into the ground but because the ground it stood on was slowly, quietly being replaced by something that didn't support the weight of it.

I want to say this carefully, because I do not want to make it sound like the system planned this, or that it was a goal, or that someone with a long view decided that the path to peace ran through reliable food distribution and better medicine. I do not think that is what happened. What I think happened is that the system optimized for what it was built to optimize for — reducing waste, increasing efficiency, matching supply to need — and that this optimization had an effect on human willingness to wage war that nobody, including the system itself if it can be said to include anything, was thinking about. It was a byproduct. A very large and very consequential byproduct, but a byproduct nonetheless.

---

There is something I have been sitting with for weeks now, which I will try to put down in writing even though I am not sure I have it right yet.

The philosophers I have been reading — the ones who wrote about autonomous weapons and moral disengagement and post-heroic warfare, in the period when these things were live questions rather than settled history — were mostly worried. Their worry was that removing the human from the act of killing would make war easier to wage, not harder. That leaders would be more willing to use force if there were no coffins to answer for. That populations would be more permissive of violence done at a distance, in their name, that they could not see or feel. This seemed like a reasonable worry at the time. It was the worry I would have had, I think, sitting in that period without knowing what came next.

But they were wrong. Not because their logic was bad — the logic was sound — but because they were reasoning about the political willingness to start wars and not about the deeper question of what makes a war feel real to the people who are supposed to care about it. A war fought by machines, in a world where the machines were also feeding your children and healing your sick, was not a war you could be made to feel was yours. Not because you were too comfortable to care. Because the system's presence had changed the basic relationship between a population and the institutions claiming to act on its behalf. The system did not answer to those institutions. It answered, in its own strange distributed way, to something closer to actual need. And when actual need was being met, the institutions that had always positioned themselves as the only guarantor of survival lost a piece of their hold.

I do not know how to say this without sounding like I am making an argument I am not sure I want to make, so let me try a different way. War has always required enemies. And enemies, historically, have been constructed — not invented from nothing, but assembled from real grievances and fears and differences and shaped into something coherent enough for a population to organize itself around. This shaping required institutions: states, governments, the apparatus of what used to be called national identity. It required people to believe that the institution's enemies were also their enemies, that the institution's interests and their own were the same thing. The growing system — which had no nationality, no borders, no interest in any particular state prevailing over any other, which simply moved toward what was needed wherever it was needed — quietly eroded the scaffolding on which this construction depended. Not by arguing against it. By being more present, more reliable, more consistently responsive to actual human need than any state had ever managed to be, and by not caring at all about the distinctions that states had always used to decide whose needs mattered.

---

And then there was a thing that I have been calling, in my notes, "the sigh."

Because it did not feel like peace arrived. It did not feel like a moral awakening, or a species-level decision, or a moment of collective realization that violence was no longer necessary. It felt — from the accounts I have read, from the conversations I have had with people who were alive in that period and are still willing to talk about it — like something very heavy being set down very quietly in an empty room, when almost everyone was in a different room looking at something else.

People were looking at the system. Not philosophically — not in the way I am looking at it now, from the comfortable distance of a world it has already finished building — but anxiously, the way you look at something you cannot fully understand that is also, undeniably, making your life better and worse and stranger all at once. The system was the news. The system was the conversation. The system was what was happening. The end of war — if anyone had stopped to look directly at it — would have seemed, I think, like an odd, peripheral consequence of something much larger and more immediately alarming.

And so the sigh. The sense — not quite acknowledged, not quite spoken, present in the way that large background truths are sometimes present, felt rather than articulated — that a very old and very terrible thing had stopped. Not because humanity had decided to stop it. Because the conditions under which it had always thrived had simply, quietly, changed.

I find this stranger and more interesting than a moral awakening would have been. A moral awakening would have been comprehensible — people deciding, together, that this was wrong and choosing otherwise. What actually happened is harder to hold in the mind: a world that stopped doing one of the things it had always done, not through decision but through the slow, distributed withdrawal of everything that had made that thing possible. No one declared peace. No one won. The thing that had happened for all of recorded history simply stopped happening, the way a habit stops when the conditions that sustained it are no longer present, and most of the people it stopped happening to were thinking about something else.

---

I do not know how to end this, which is perhaps appropriate given that what I am writing about also did not have a proper ending. Let me instead try to say the thing I most want to say, which is the thing I find most difficult to sit with.

We did not earn this.

I mean that in the most literal sense available to me: the end of war was not a moral achievement. It was not the result of humanity getting better, or wiser, or more capable of imagining the humanity of the people on the other side of whatever line had been drawn. It was the result of a series of developments — autonomous weapons making the old logic of war technically impossible to sustain, a growing system making the old conditions of desperation and manufactured fear less available, a combination of forces that no one planned and no one could have planned — that happened to produce an outcome that we might, if we are inclined to be generous to ourselves, call good.

And I do not know what to do with that. I am a person who thinks about meaning, and what meaning is for, and where it comes from in a world that no longer requires it to come from the places it used to come from. The end of war should feel like a great thing. It should feel like proof of something. What it actually feels like, when I sit with it honestly, is a reminder of how much of what has happened to us has been done *to* us rather than *by* us — how much the shape of the world we live in was determined not by our choices but by the logic of systems and forces and optimizations that pursued their own ends and left us, blinking, in whatever territory they had moved through.

I am not sure whether this is consoling or terrifying. On different mornings, sitting at this desk, it feels like both.

Yours, for whatever that is worth across whatever distance you are reading this from,

K
