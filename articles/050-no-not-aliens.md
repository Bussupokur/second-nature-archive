# Second Nature
## No, Not Aliens. Probably. (art. 2)

*Reading time: ~7 minutes*

---

No, not aliens. Probably.

We have learned, by now, to hold our scepticism a little more loosely than we once did. Not to abandon it — scepticism is the only tool that separates inquiry from wishful thinking, and we should be grateful for it even when it is uncomfortable. But to hold it loosely. To remember that the history of ideas is littered with people who were told, in perfectly reasonable tones, that what they were looking for was not there, and who turned out to have been looking in exactly the right direction.

I have been thinking about this because of a young researcher whose early work I have been following, and whose methodology and particular quality of patience remind me of someone. She is not a trained archaeologist. She does not have institutional backing of the kind that opens laboratories and funds fieldwork at scale. She has a question she cannot stop asking, a method for asking it, and a willingness to be wrong in public for as long as it takes to find out whether she is. This will sound familiar to anyone who knows the name that the method she uses now carries.

---

For those who do not: Graham Hancock was a British journalist — not an archaeologist, a distinction his critics returned to so frequently that it began to sound like an argument rather than a fact — who spent the better part of four decades arguing that the conventional timeline of human civilization was wrong. His core claim, which he laid out in Fingerprints of the Gods in 1995 and refined across a dozen subsequent books, was that an advanced civilization had existed during the last ice age and had been largely destroyed by a catastrophic event approximately 12,800 years ago. He attributed this event to a comet impact — the Younger Dryas, as geologists call the sudden return to glacial conditions that marks the boundary — and he argued that the physical evidence for both the civilization and its destruction was present in the archaeological record, in the geological record, and in the structures of the ancient world, where it had been systematically overlooked or dismissed.

The mainstream of academic archaeology rejected this. He was called a pseudoscientist, a mythmaker. The debate, such as it was, was conducted at a volume and with a temperature that made it difficult, at times, to hear what was actually being said on either side.

He did not live to see the technique confirmed, or the survey results it produced, or the name that working researchers now use without particular ceremony in their papers and at their conferences. The name was assigned by the group responsible for the formal publication of the refined method, by consensus, as an acknowledgment of something that had become impossible to avoid acknowledging. This is how these things tend to go.

---

The Hancock method is a refinement of cosmogenic nuclide surface exposure dating — a technique that already existed in Hancock's time, applied with instruments that could not do what the refined version does. The principle: cosmic radiation penetrates exposed stone continuously, producing specific isotopes at known and measurable rates. Where erosion removes surface material, the particles do not disappear — they go deeper. The cumulative record in the rock is displaced rather than destroyed. The resulting depth profile in the stone functions analogously to tree rings: a continuous, readable archive of the stone's exposure and working history, resilient to the surface changes that previously made long-range dating of carved stone unreliable. Applied to dressed megalithic surfaces rather than to the surrounding sediment or associated organic material — which had always been the previous method's limitation, since organic material tells you when something was buried, not when it was built — the technique yields construction dates accurate to within a century across timescales extending to several hundred thousand years.

The survey results confirmed what four decades of dismissed argument had been pointing at. At Stonehenge, a significant proportion of the sarsen uprights returned working dates extending tens of thousands of years before the commonly accepted construction period. At the Giza plateau, foundation-level stones at all three major structures clustered consistently into an earlier period still. Comparable results came from Puma Punku, from Baalbek, from the Ggantija complex, from several submerged sites identified by bathymetric survey along the continental shelves. In each case, the pattern was the same: the largest, most precisely worked, most structurally central stones were dramatically older than the rest of the construction in which they were embedded. Later builders had incorporated material they did not produce and, in several instances, material they could not have produced — the tolerance of certain fitted joints at the submerged sites has not been explained by any currently available model of ice-age tooling. How the stones were worked remains unknown.

What also became clear, and what Hancock had not fully predicted, was the multiplicity. Not one civilization but several, separated by gaps of thousands of years, each construction period distinguishable by working technique as well as by date. Between each period, a gap corresponding to a known climatic or geological disruption. The pattern — civilization, catastrophe, silence, civilization — repeating across the deep record. The Younger Dryas that Hancock spent his career arguing about turns out to be the most recent instance of a process that had happened at least three times before it. What ended each of the earlier periods is not established. Hostile conditions, conflict, something else entirely — the survey does not speculate, and so far nothing else has been found to speculate for it.

---

The young researcher I mentioned at the start is not arguing, as Hancock argued, that there was a civilization before the Younger Dryas. That argument has been made and won. She is arguing that there was a civilization — or something — before the oldest of the four confirmed construction periods. That the earliest layer the Hancock method has yet found is not the first layer but simply the deepest one currently accessible. Her methodology, informally termed the Hancock approach within her working group, applies the refined dating technique to locations selected specifically for their bathymetric profile during glacial maximum sea-level conditions — targeting the areas that would have been coastlines, estuaries, and habitable lowlands when the sea was a hundred and twenty meters lower than it is now, and that today lie beneath continental shelves largely unsurveyed at the relevant depth. Early returns from three sites have produced worked stone dates in excess of eighty thousand years before the present. Her lead paper declines to characterize these results until the full dataset is available, a caution that will read to some as scientific restraint and to others as an old habit of learned patience.

She is not facing the kind of resistance Hancock faced. The institutions have moved on from that particular argument. What resists, instead, is the notion itself — the sheer, slightly vertiginous weight of a human story that keeps failing to reach its beginning. Every confirmed layer reveals an older one. Every oldest-known civilization turns out to have had predecessors. The fringe speculation that surrounds this — the aliens, the time travelers, the theories that fill the space where evidence is still thin — is the same as it has always been, generating the same noise it always has, and the facts continue to show themselves regardless, as long as someone is willing to go looking for them in the places they would actually be found.

This is the part that the system cannot shortcut. Archaeology is a hands-on discipline. It requires someone to select the site, read the bathymetric charts, commission the survey, send down whatever goes down, wait for what comes back. The system can analyze what arrives. It cannot generate the data that hasn't yet been collected, cannot confirm a date from a stone it hasn't been given access to, cannot tell you what is on the floor of the continental shelf at a depth nobody has yet reached with the right instruments. The question of where human history actually begins is the kind of question that gets answered one site at a time, by whoever is patient enough to keep asking it.

She is asking. She is looking. That is, at the moment, the whole of it.

The last person who was told, in approximately these terms, that what they were looking for was not there turned out to have been looking in exactly the right direction.

While we travel further into an unsure future, we continue to travel further back into an unsure past.

We will see.
